Why we should stop pursuing happiness, and what we should do instead

Sociologist Eva Illouz’s book about the ‘happiness industry’ assails positive psychology and the coaching culture, which place human happiness at the center

Alon Idan
05.05.2022

לחצו כאן

Was it even a conversation about happiness? I’m not sure. We talked about the subject, but it’s unlikely that it was present when I met with Eva Illouz in the cafeteria of the Maiersdorf Faculty Club on the Mount Scopus campus of the Hebrew University. Maybe because happiness doesn’t really exist, maybe because it’s impossible to touch it with words, and maybe because it wasn’t a conversation between two happy people, at least not in the marketing-commercial-New Age sense of the word.

We met in the wake of the publication in Hebrew of the book “Manufacturing Happy Citizens: How the Science and Industry of Happiness Control Our Lives” (English edition published in 2019 by Polity Books), which Illouz co-wrote with the Spanish critical psychologist Edgar Cabanas. The book attempts to delineate the way in which happiness has become an engineered product whose role is to subordinate the individual to the prodigious economic machine of capitalism.

The infrastructure for this type of happiness is provided by positive psychology, which with its bastard sister, the coaching industry, turns over billions of dollars a year. Yet they are based, pseudo-scientifically, on happiness indices that were introduced in order “to check whether, despite the continuing decline of objective indexes of quality of life and [social] equality, people were still nonetheless feeling well,” as the book notes.

It’s nothing short of a pandemic. In England and France, the bureaus of statistics collect information about their citizens’ happiness, drawing on the notion of a “gross happiness index.” The United Nations began publishing a World Happiness Report and declared March 20 the International Day of Happiness. Naturally, international corporations, among them Coca-Cola, spotted the potential and began marketing a “happiness machine” (it hasn’t yet been announced whether Coke Zero will be sold in happiness machines).

As to the question of whether there is actually such a thing as happiness – even before the manipulations that big money seeks to foment – that’s something Illouz finds it difficult to answer. She’s a sociologist, so from her point of view, it’s not her job “to ask how things like love or happiness ‘should be.’” Pressed, she explains that “historically, happiness was usually defined in proximity to what Aristotle would have called the virtues of character. With most philosophers you don’t see the possibility that a human being will be bad and happy. And I would like to preserve that restrictive condition.”

You want there to be a correlation between morality and justice, and happiness.

“Between happiness and the virtues. The virtues can also include emotions that are not perceived as positive, for example the possibility of becoming angry. At the appropriate time, the appropriate moment, one needs to be able to be angry.”

In this book, her anger is aimed at the father of what is known as positive psychology, Martin Seligman, an American psychologist of 79 who – there’s no nice way to say this – simply drives her up a wall. “He sees himself as the Einstein of the brain, of the psyche. He says that until now all of psychology and psychoanalysis have been wrong, because their point of departure was the suffering person. And he says that suffering doesn’t interest him, he’s interested only in the positive elements in people.”

Here, then, is the essence of the freak-out: After many years during which traditional psychology did everything it could to map human suffering and to reduce it, there arrives a highly regarded psychologist – a former president of the American Psychological Association – and shouts into the mic: Guys, enough already with the gloomy look, let’s go for fulfillment, let’s grow, let’s be happy, go for it – a revolution of joy.

But this joy has a dark side – it’s called “personal responsibility.” For Seligman and for positive psychology, people bear almost exclusive personal responsibility for their employment, their economic situation and their mental state. They can make themselves successful and happy, or they can relinquish responsibility and fall into a chasm of darkness. The political, cultural and economic context within which people act become becomes a thin backdrop of no real consequence. “A problem is created,” Illouz tells me, “because you deny that there is a reality that exists outside the individual – a social, economic, political reality – over which you exercise very little influence. The result is to transfer the only reality there is inward, into the person.”

If one has to define what “Eva Illouz” is, it would be the absolute, deep rejection of the transition that culture is trying to advance from occupation with the external, political world to occupation with the internal, psychological one. Illouz’s whole project – happiness is only one link in a chain of major human emotions, such as love and suffering, that she studies – is engaged in an attempt to show that this transition is destructive and brings into being a distorted world rife with vast socioeconomic disparities within which zombies driving jeeps are preoccupied all day with the question of “how do you feel about it.”

How does it manifest in practice, this self-absorption?

“‘It all depends on you.’ That sentence. ‘It all depends on how you think about it.’”

It’s all in the head.

“‘All in the head’ – exactly. I remember that once I took part in a three-day seminar in which you had to stand in front of 200 people and say what causes you pain, and that ‘I commit today to change this and this.’”

Awful. How could you do something like that?

“I couldn’t do it, I didn’t make it. I had a feeling of being in a cult, that they were trying almost by force to alter people’s way of thinking and their perception of suffering: Everything bad stems from the fact that you perceive it as bad.”

At this stage she offers a long example from the book that can be summarized as follows: Two employees were fired. One – hereafter the “bad example” – dropped out of the labor market and descended into a spiral of self-pity and depression, while the other “believed in” himself, bridged the “temporary regression” – for positive psychologists, every regression is temporary – and rehabilitated his life.

That means that the flip side of “it’s all up to you” is “you’re on your own if you’re fired – you loser!”

“That’s the second thing I wanted to tell you. Control of the question of what causes what, is political. Is unemployment a result of personal laziness, or is it caused by corporations that are constantly trying to streamline? The concept of responsibility is very important, but the moment you say, at the collective level, that most of the bad things that happen are due to the fact that people don’t manage themselves well, you are effectively neutralizing political thought. That is a danger to democracy. What does positive psychology do? It tells individuals that the real world lies within, inside them. Don’t look outside, the outside is just a reflection of who you are. The only thing you can influence is yourself, and that’s it.”

That is a critique that you have put forward in different forms over the years, not only in the context of positive psychology. But still, if it’s so awful, why is psychology so successful?

“One thing is that liberal democracies are very complex societies. We’re talking [these days] about individuals who are supposedly equal to each other – husband and wife, for example. A hundred years ago, the husband was entitled to tell the wife what to do. There wasn’t much negotiation or flexibility. But when marriage becomes democratic, egalitarian, it’s a constant negotiation: The question of who does what is there all the time. The individual needs to clarify to himself what he wants and how he will avoid conflict. The stricter the prohibition on wielding authority, the stronger psychology becomes. It’s a very effective system in that it helps with comporting oneself in the world of negotiations. That’s one thing.”

Meaning there is a second thing.

“The second thing is that, if there is one element that characterizes modern life, it’s the fact that my identity is not a given – it too is in negotiation. Sociologists call it a society of evaluation. I need to choose a job, what to study, a place to live, a partner; life is a series of choices of which only the subject is the source. Today the individual is in an almost constant state of uncertainty.”

Uncertainty gives rise to anxiety.

Illouz's desire to connect ‘private happiness’ to ‘the general good’ – and, to be precise: to sever the possibility that an egoistical person will feel great and terrific and perfect – recalls many religious concepts in which the idea that 'the wicked thrive' is a dissonance that merits an outcry and demands an explanation from God.

“Uncertainty doesn’t square with people’s basic wiring. In a situation where people have to clarify nonstop who they are and what they want and what the right choice is, the psychologists are there as a tool of clarification and of honing and shaping identity.”

But your complaint about psychology doesn’t stop with the honing of identity.

“People have good reasons to go into therapy, because the conditions of modern existence are complex and also uncertain. But psychology tends to entrench your identity. What happened is that psychology from one side and identity politics from the other side have created an individual who is obsessive about their identity – be it sexual, gender, religious or ethnic.”

How does having an entrenched identity help one to conduct negotiations with the other in order to resolve conflicts?

“Ironically, people who have undergone therapy often develop boundaries that are less flexible. Effectively, the individual is far less capable of negotiating with others because of these shaped boundaries of identity. Another key reason for psychology’s attractiveness is that our self-worth is constantly being tested – in admission to school, in being hired, on dates, when you use a dating app.”

Tinder.

“Tinder, exactly. We are being tested all the time. You are now testing me, and I you. Society is in a situation of perpetual testing, so socially, our self-worth is the most important thing. You can upgrade your worth, elevate it. The whole occupation with self-worth stems from the modern conditions of existence. The psychologists’ role is to rehabilitate that worth and to build it up, when so many institutions are undermining it.”

And then positive psychology goes a step further by not making do with raising self-worth, but also wants you to be happy.

“Let’s talk for a moment about one of Seligman’s ideas, called ‘post-traumatic growth.’ That means, I was raped or I killed someone in war or a friend of mine was killed in war – in any event, I’ve undergone a trauma. The imperative of positive psychology is not only to overcome the trauma, but also to grow from it and develop internal resilience.”

What’s bad about growing?

“One of the great things about psychoanalysis and psychology is their focus on mental suffering and the fact that they have demarcated a detailed map of personal disorders or mental processes that cause us to suffer. Seligman rejects completely the importance of suffering and its treatment. He declares that he needs to take from the psyche only the mechanisms that will encourage positive thought and will give a person a sense of empowerment.”

A “sense of capability.”

“And that capability is supposed to lead us to eliminating the ability of a traumatic event to shatter the psyche. So I hope I answered you.”

You answered, but…

“Another significant difference is that, politically, it’s a quantum leap. In general, from the beginning, psychology entered a great many realms in society: manager-employee relations in corporations, prisons and rehabilitation programs, schools. What didn’t happen was an entry into the economy in that form. Seligman succeeded in creating a sub-field, in the economy of happiness, which claims that misery should be measured in terms of money. Happiness brings in money, misery costs you money. That’s a quantum leap.”

But it could be that after 100 years during which psychology functioned as an industry that dealt with human suffering, a stage arrived in which the culture wanted to deal with human happiness.

“Look, I see that it’s… No, no, I’m trying to find the right wording.”

That happens occasionally, those pauses. At first they seem related to language issues. Illouz is compelled to toggle between a slightly broken Hebrew and French and English. Subsequently the impression changes. The stoppages are related also, and perhaps principally, to her desire to think about her answers before replying. Not infrequently, she also admits that she doesn’t have a good answer to a question or that she’s ambivalent about it. In a culture of television panel discussions in which replies are well-worn configurations of declarations of talking points, there’s something refreshing about the fact that it’s not an automatic response that’s sitting opposite you.

“All religions and a large part of Western philosophy share the conception that the psyche needs shaping and good education,” she continues. “There is no good society if you don’t have good citizens. And good citizens need to possess a worthy psyche. Because of that, in my view, the psychological discussion cannot be disconnected from a more general discussion about the virtues.”

Illouz’s recurring desire to connect “private happiness” to “the general good” – and, to be precise: to sever the possibility that an egoistical person will feel great and terrific and perfect – recalls many religious concepts in which the idea that “the wicked thrive” is a dissonance that merits an outcry and demands an explanation from God. When Illouz insists on the affinity between “happiness” and the values of justice, equality and compassion, one can only imagine millions of patients in psychologists’ clinics recalling in a broken voice their early childhood and the formative moment when their mother forgot to pick them up from kindergarten, when suddenly Illouz enters the frame dramatically and scolds them: You really have had a hard time, but that doesn’t give you the license to behave in life as though only you exist!

“You asked why in effect this notion of happiness should be rejected. I don’t want to reject it. We do not want unhappy people. But I also think that there are parts of the psyche that Spinoza called ‘sad passions,’ such as envy or melancholy, and on the other hand, good passions such as happiness. We are living in an almost anti-Spinozist era, in which everything that exists I can and am even duty-bound to change. With Spinoza, one needs the ability to understand that these sad passions are a result of inflation of the self or of an incorrect understanding of the world. That too is a program of happiness, but very different.”

It’s definitely different from Coca-Cola’s happiness workshops, or from Facebook, and also from Google, which takes pride in its ultra-cool employment conditions. So they tell you, bring the dog with you to work, play ping-pong, go home when you feel like it, and on top of all that, you’ll get a high salary. Sounds good, all in all.

“The problem is that the workplace tries to shape the employees’ entire subjectivity. Modernity was based on a sharp separation between work, on the one hand, and family and the private sphere; and the more they were separated, the better it was for both spheres. What we have been witnessing in the past 30 years is that the employee has come to be identified according to such vague qualities as creativity and personality. The workplace increasingly assumed the authority to shape the employees’ emotional structure from within, and to demand in return a particular emotional structure, largely positive. That means that the workplace is fusing more and more with the private sphere.”

But maybe people would like to see that fusion happen. Maybe people are no longer interested in separation.

“Look, I’m not sure I have a good answer to that. I will say this: When there are two sides in the interaction, my sociological tendency is to say that if there is one side that is dramatically stronger, the interaction takes place, apparently because it serves that side. But I also agree with you: There is truly an exchange eros.”

Meaning?

“I think that the workplace has become the new eros. Many people’s identity is fulfilled by their work. In a certain sense, the workplace is more rewarding, more organized, the rules are clearer, there are multiple interactions. It’s a more aesthetic world: I dress well, I put on makeup, that means more stimuli. The workplace is becoming increasingly people’s central source of identity. What does that mean in practice? Because, to say ‘work’ sounds like a fine word, but it means that Facebook becomes your source of identity. Meaning that Google, or Coca-Cola, becomes our source of identity.”

People drive a company car with a sticker that says, “How am I driving?” The company car and I are one.

“I use buses and taxis.”

The plasticity of personality

Illouz (60, three children, not married) was born in Morocco, grew up in France, obtained a doctorate in the United States (communications and cultural studies, University of Pennsylvania), and arrived in Israel in 1991. She’s considered a sociological success story: She received a prize from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem for outstanding research, was awarded the title of Knight of the Legion of Honor in France in 2019, was invited to deliver the Adorno lecture series in Germany, holds the Rose Isaac Chair in Sociology at the Hebrew University, is a senior research fellow at both the Van Leer Jerusalem Institute and Hebrew University’s Center for the Study of Rationality, and is the author of 13 books (and is currently completing two more).

Nevertheless, at one point, I wondered whether Illouz wasn’t somewhat trapped in a fixed structure. She takes from humanity the great emotions – love, suffering, and now happiness – exposing them as synthetic products of the period and tracing the capitalist wiring that leads to all those “vested interests.”

The moment you say, at the collective level, that most of the bad things that happen are due to the fact that people don’t manage themselves well, you are effectively neutralizing political thought. That’s a danger to democracy.Illouz

“Because politics in my eyes is to give a name to suffering and to connect between my suffering, or say, that of my neighbor, and something that a different group is doing that is causing that suffering,” she says. “The political sphere must be able to allow for the possibility of building groups and even alliances between groups with very different identities. Suffering has undergone a process of privatization. Think of Betty Friedan, for example. When she wrote ‘The Feminine Mystique,’ she described a ‘problem that has no name,’ that’s what she called it. Namely, a situation in which women in those pseudo-American houses feel a great discomfort and blame themselves. She triggered a huge explosion, because she gave it a name – the situation of the woman within society. Our place is not in the house, polishing the floor and waiting for husband to come home. There is always a struggle between forces that want to privatize suffering and forces that are trying to give it a name, and not only to give it a name, but to point to a broad group that feels the same way.”

To translate this into Israeli: Think of the Israeli high-tech person who, though living in an “accessorized” mansion and having already booked a summer vacation in the Seychelles, also, on the other hand, works 12 hours a day, travels two hours in traffic jams, does too much reserve duty and pays too much in taxes. When that person feels pressure that turns into quarrels with his partner or into aggressive behavior in the public space, is the answer that “it’s all in the head” legitimate? Is therapy that analyzes “his place” in the family during very early childhood, or a quarter-hour of daily mindfulness meditation, really the solution?

“Everyone who is engaged in the pursuit of happiness thinks that the reason they’re not succeeding is their parents, who didn’t give them enough attention, who didn’t encourage them enough, or sometimes over-encouraged them,” Illouz says. “In other words, everything and its opposite becomes a source for explaining why I’m not succeeding, whereas in fact, the modern conditions of existence create wellbeing for only a small group. That is why I say that the language of psychology is changing the grounds of reality. The reality we live and feel today is the inner reality of what others have done to us and [the question of] how I can correct that from within myself. Politics requires a type of suspension of a conception like that, a type of detachment from the inner self, it calls for looking outward and identifying a common object. It is the identification of that common object that is missing so badly.”

So instead of politics we get coaching: Do it this way, do it differently.

“Yes, you have a task and now I am helping you solve a specific problem. Like positive psychology, coaching also believes in the plasticity of the personality, in the ability to change – both offer techniques for how to change. It’s you with yourself and with practices you need to do.”

A gym.

“Exactly.”

Maybe that’s why it’s called “coaching.”

“Yes, it’s a different conceptualization of the psyche.”

But maybe people are fed up with all the talk about mother, father, childhood? Maybe it shows that people are tired of classic psychology? “Just tell me what to do” – and that’s it.

“I would say that it perhaps shows that psychology, like coaching, also provides techniques for self-management. There is sometimes a very lovely and very impressive theoretical envelope in psychology, but when you discard that whole envelope, you are actually encouraging people to move away from their habits and patterns.”

Now she talks about the American historian T.J. Jackson Lears (“He’s fantastic”), whose 1981 book “No Place of Grace” (“a work of genius”) deals with the process in which, according to Illouz, “a person’s whole external envelope changes completely. Suddenly you see a lot of light in the streets, glowing billboards, films, media, luxurious display windows. The external façade of the world changes and changes the conception of reality. Since then we have entered a new phase, the virtual world, which renders even more extreme the feeling of loss of the concreteness of reality, so that the movement inward of people is also increasing. A feeling is being created that the external world, which is so saturated with images and pictures, is not real.”

One of the most common expressions of this today is the phrase “you’re not real.”

“Yes. And when the external world becomes so acutely disjointed, what really remains to you? You.”

And if so, it’s important for that “you” to be as authentic as possible.

“Authenticity is one of the most productive concepts, in the sense that it generates multiple economic and mental practicalities. For example, at the economic level, the ‘authentic product’ will always cost more. Authenticity is a marketing strategy that increases significantly the value of the product. Think of ‘authentic [ethnic] restaurants.’”

But what does it mean in the case of a person who wants to feel authentic?

“I’m ambivalent here, because the concept of authenticity expresses moral advancement. Take Ibsen’s plays, for example: They are filled with female characters who lead false lives from which they are trying to liberate themselves. Or Anna Karenina, for example. Obviously we all admire the fact that she is trying to live her life differently from the formulas and conventions her husband obeys. On the other hand, authenticity has become like Achilles in Zeno’s paradox.”

You’re constantly moving toward the goal, but you’ll never reach it.

“Abraham Maslow [the American psychologist who developed the humanistic approach] was among the first who put forward the idea of self-actualization. That notion draws a division between the life I have now and the life I could have, if only I knew how to eliminate all the inner barriers that are stopping me. Maslow also sees self-actualization as social success. How will I know that I have achieved self-actualization? By the fact that I will reach the summit. But there can be very few people at the summit, so it follows that not everyone can achieve self-actualization. Accordingly, that idea is false. The consumer culture, from the torn jeans to organic vegetables and a vacation in nature, also promises to connect us to a hidden part of ourselves.”

When people say they want to be authentic, what they mean is that they want to behave outwardly the way they feel and think inwardly.

“I think that what’s termed authentic is a variety of things. For example, if you are in a mediocre marriage, it’s very easy for you to say, ‘I am not living my authentic love in my marriage.’ What the British psychoanalyst Adam Phillips calls ‘the unlived life.’ We live in the shadow of the life we could be living.”

And in practice what happens?

“Our life is filled with contradictions and is bland.”

Or maybe it’s what we don’t want to accept at the underlying base: day-to-day life when it is not filled with thrills and achievements.

“So at this point I want to bring in the concept of the aestheticization of life. Aestheticization constitutes an important dimension of modern life; for example, media, movies and commercials present a beautiful body, characters that live in literary genres are defined well, with a beginning, a middle and an end. What does ‘aesthetic’ mean? It means that we imagine our life as though it were a novel. An aesthetic dimension is one in which everything has a place and has meaning. Now, life is not aesthetic. It contains an amorphous sequence of events that don’t exactly have logic between them. There is a very wide gulf between the aesthetic consciousness of life, which is constantly growing, and the ability to live a bland life that is filled with constraints.”

You see that aestheticization in Instagram, where everything is beautiful, everything is delightful.

“And all that is part of your self-staging. And happiness, I’m afraid, is also part of the same self-staging.

Entering the happiness machine

'Aesthetic' means that we imagine our life as though it were a novel. An aesthetic dimension is one in which everything has a place and has meaning. Now, life is not aesthetic.Illouz

The book about happiness sprang from an article Illouz published in Le Monde about the concept of “resilience.” “Seligman began to study depression in the 1970s,” she relates. “He conducted experiments in which electric shocks were administered to a group of dogs. One group could press a button and put a stop to the shocks, a second group could not. A few weeks later, the first group was able to reprise the experiment, while the second group wasn’t able to [stop the electric shocks].”

Sounds logical.

“But Seligman coined a very interesting phrase in the wake of the experiment: ‘learned helplessness.’ The group that learned that it was impossible to stop the suffering, stopped trying to overcome it. That could have been a fantastic psychological finding on the way to understanding why people give up in advance. But Seligman didn’t take it to that place. He conducted similar experiments on humans, using noise instead of electric shocks. He noticed that in the group that was supposed to learn helplessness, there were a few individuals who nevertheless managed to turn off the terrible noise. He took an interest in them, in the exceptions.”

They are the source of positive psychology.

“Exactly – that’s where it originated. And then he asks himself why these people are actually insisting on trying. And there he coins the term ‘resilience.’ Meaning that no matter what happens to people, how unlucky they may be, they are stubborn. And from that group he drew an inference for the rest.”

But what are the traits of the people who didn’t despair? Maybe they are simply obsessive people who repeat the same action over and over, with no connection to what’s happening around them? Because there is actually something natural about concluding, after you have tried and tried unsuccessfully, that it’s apparently impossible.

“What you are saying in a different way is also something that [I am] saying. Namely, that possibly what these positive traits indicate is the existence of a type of impenetrability: that a resilient person is effectively an impenetrable person. And there is no way you can distinguish between the good, positive person and the impenetrable, disconnected person.”

So maybe the world is developing emotional detachment as some sort of mass mechanism that will make it possible to survive modern life?

“Yes, I think that there is such a tendency and such a danger. The problem with the concept of emotional resilience is that you aren’t able to distinguish between mental resilience and disconnect.”

But maybe that disconnect has advantages. Maybe those who can disconnect themselves from negative feelings and remain only with the positive ones prefers this option?

“Robert Nozick [the late American philosopher] conducted an interesting conceptual exercise. He said we should imagine a happiness machine that people are put into, and in which they can imagine whatever they want for 80 years. For example, that you’re married to the most beautiful woman and that you are the most successful author and that your life is wonderful. Nozick wanted to say, ‘Well, obviously we won’t choose a life like that,’ we would prefer a real, less happy, life. But it seems to me that at the collective level we are less and less capable of making the distinction and of wanting to make the distinction, between an imagined life and imagined happiness and the real thing.”

Meaning that there are people who would say, “Let’s do it – put me in the happiness machine.”

“I think there will be more like that. In other words, possibly we have exchanged intuition; it changed because of the virtual world. It’s like asking whether some who takes drugs and gets a good feeling from it, is doing something proper or improper. I don’t really have an answer, I would say that it depends on your philosophy of happiness. If you believe that there should be some sort of meaning that is outside you and outside the feelings you experience, and if you believe in the substantiality of the world, then it’s not enough.”

What do you mean by the “substantiality of the world”?

“For me it means that there is always something that is resisting my desire or my wish. That is the substantiality of the world.”

Does there always have to be something that’s resisting me?

“It means that you are born into a world that cannot fulfill all your needs, your desires. This occupies a central place throughout Lacan’s psychoanalysis. He says that lack is the essence of desire, it is absolutely part of humanity’s being. So the question is whether you think it’s possible to live without that lack. I think not.”

Positive psychology tries to erase that lack.

“Completely. It doesn’t try – it erases it. It rejects it out of hand.”

But after all the brainwashing, is it really possible to erase that lack in people?

“You can deny the existence of the lack very cleverly.”

So, when someone reports that they don’t feel the lack?

“The prevailing ideology of positiveness and happiness compels us not to report it.”

But some people do report it. Are they lying?

“Clearly there is a situation in which a person feels in harmony with their life. About people like that, as Tolstoy would note, I don’t have much to say. But one of Freud’s central concepts, and I very much believe in them, is denial. In the first stage, in the psyche, and afterward at the political level. So, in order to distinguish between a false report about happiness and an appropriate report, I would ask about the degree of effort involved in the experience of the denial.”

Please explain.

“If a person makes an effort to deny, invests mental energy, I would say that the happy identity there is imposed or borrowed, also at the collective level.”

On the broiling, empty porch of the cafeteria, with a spectacular view of the Old City spread out in front of us, Illouz mentions “a wonderful novel that I am wild about, ‘The Remains of the Day’ [by Kazuo Ishiguro]. There is the character of the butler, Mr. Stevens, who is totally characterized by the effort to deny. The narrator lets us understand what the character does not grasp. Now, the butler lives his life obediently, carrying out his role as a butler meticulously, apart from one small moment in which he understands the great lost opportunity of his life. But very quickly his consciousness shuts down again. For me, moments of disillusionment like that, despite the inherent pain, are worth more than happiness, or at least not less than it.”

So a moment before his consciousness shuts down, would you say to him, “Take note, you are living in denial,” or would you prefer to let him go insular and return to his previous life?

“In a letter that Freud wrote to his future wife, Martha, in 1883, he says: Let’s be truthful – psychoanalysis is only for people of means. He writes that the working class, if we were to open its consciousness, would not be able to profit from that consciousness, because its conditions of existence will remain very limited. Freud says that we don’t want to do that.”

So we don’t want to?

“It’s a text that amazed me when I read it. Because Freud effectively recognized that class differences exist and that opening the consciousness can be useful only for an established class; in other words, that a disillusioned consciousness is meaningful only if the conditions of existence allow something to be done with it. To open the consciousness of someone who doesn’t have anything to do with that consciousness is neither useful nor moral.”

So, do you tell him or not?

“It’s a dilemma.”

Was it even a conversation about happiness? I’m not sure. We talked about the subject, but it’s unlikely that it was present when I met with Eva Illouz in the cafeteria of the Maiersdorf Faculty Club on the Mount Scopus campus of the Hebrew University. Maybe because happiness doesn’t really exist, maybe because it’s impossible to touch it with words, and maybe because it wasn’t a conversation between two happy people, at least not in the marketing-commercial-New Age sense of the word.

We met in the wake of the publication in Hebrew of the book “Manufacturing Happy Citizens: How the Science and Industry of Happiness Control Our Lives” (English edition published in 2019 by Polity Books), which Illouz co-wrote with the Spanish critical psychologist Edgar Cabanas. The book attempts to delineate the way in which happiness has become an engineered product whose role is to subordinate the individual to the prodigious economic machine of capitalism.

The infrastructure for this type of happiness is provided by positive psychology, which with its bastard sister, the coaching industry, turns over billions of dollars a year. Yet they are based, pseudo-scientifically, on happiness indices that were introduced in order “to check whether, despite the continuing decline of objective indexes of quality of life and [social] equality, people were still nonetheless feeling well,” as the book notes.

It’s nothing short of a pandemic. In England and France, the bureaus of statistics collect information about their citizens’ happiness, drawing on the notion of a “gross happiness index.” The United Nations began publishing a World Happiness Report and declared March 20 the International Day of Happiness. Naturally, international corporations, among them Coca-Cola, spotted the potential and began marketing a “happiness machine” (it hasn’t yet been announced whether Coke Zero will be sold in happiness machines).

As to the question of whether there is actually such a thing as happiness – even before the manipulations that big money seeks to foment – that’s something Illouz finds it difficult to answer. She’s a sociologist, so from her point of view, it’s not her job “to ask how things like love or happiness ‘should be.’” Pressed, she explains that “historically, happiness was usually defined in proximity to what Aristotle would have called the virtues of character. With most philosophers you don’t see the possibility that a human being will be bad and happy. And I would like to preserve that restrictive condition.”

You want there to be a correlation between morality and justice, and happiness.

“Between happiness and the virtues. The virtues can also include emotions that are not perceived as positive, for example the possibility of becoming angry. At the appropriate time, the appropriate moment, one needs to be able to be angry.”

In this book, her anger is aimed at the father of what is known as positive psychology, Martin Seligman, an American psychologist of 79 who – there’s no nice way to say this – simply drives her up a wall. “He sees himself as the Einstein of the brain, of the psyche. He says that until now all of psychology and psychoanalysis have been wrong, because their point of departure was the suffering person. And he says that suffering doesn’t interest him, he’s interested only in the positive elements in people.”

Here, then, is the essence of the freak-out: After many years during which traditional psychology did everything it could to map human suffering and to reduce it, there arrives a highly regarded psychologist – a former president of the American Psychological Association – and shouts into the mic: Guys, enough already with the gloomy look, let’s go for fulfillment, let’s grow, let’s be happy, go for it – a revolution of joy.

But this joy has a dark side – it’s called “personal responsibility.” For Seligman and for positive psychology, people bear almost exclusive personal responsibility for their employment, their economic situation and their mental state. They can make themselves successful and happy, or they can relinquish responsibility and fall into a chasm of darkness. The political, cultural and economic context within which people act become becomes a thin backdrop of no real consequence. “A problem is created,” Illouz tells me, “because you deny that there is a reality that exists outside the individual – a social, economic, political reality – over which you exercise very little influence. The result is to transfer the only reality there is inward, into the person.”

If one has to define what “Eva Illouz” is, it would be the absolute, deep rejection of the transition that culture is trying to advance from occupation with the external, political world to occupation with the internal, psychological one. Illouz’s whole project – happiness is only one link in a chain of major human emotions, such as love and suffering, that she studies – is engaged in an attempt to show that this transition is destructive and brings into being a distorted world rife with vast socioeconomic disparities within which zombies driving jeeps are preoccupied all day with the question of “how do you feel about it.”

How does it manifest in practice, this self-absorption?

“‘It all depends on you.’ That sentence. ‘It all depends on how you think about it.’”

It’s all in the head.

“‘All in the head’ – exactly. I remember that once I took part in a three-day seminar in which you had to stand in front of 200 people and say what causes you pain, and that ‘I commit today to change this and this.’”

Awful. How could you do something like that?

“I couldn’t do it, I didn’t make it. I had a feeling of being in a cult, that they were trying almost by force to alter people’s way of thinking and their perception of suffering: Everything bad stems from the fact that you perceive it as bad.”

At this stage she offers a long example from the book that can be summarized as follows: Two employees were fired. One – hereafter the “bad example” – dropped out of the labor market and descended into a spiral of self-pity and depression, while the other “believed in” himself, bridged the “temporary regression” – for positive psychologists, every regression is temporary – and rehabilitated his life.

That means that the flip side of “it’s all up to you” is “you’re on your own if you’re fired – you loser!”

“That’s the second thing I wanted to tell you. Control of the question of what causes what, is political. Is unemployment a result of personal laziness, or is it caused by corporations that are constantly trying to streamline? The concept of responsibility is very important, but the moment you say, at the collective level, that most of the bad things that happen are due to the fact that people don’t manage themselves well, you are effectively neutralizing political thought. That is a danger to democracy. What does positive psychology do? It tells individuals that the real world lies within, inside them. Don’t look outside, the outside is just a reflection of who you are. The only thing you can influence is yourself, and that’s it.”

That is a critique that you have put forward in different forms over the years, not only in the context of positive psychology. But still, if it’s so awful, why is psychology so successful?

“One thing is that liberal democracies are very complex societies. We’re talking [these days] about individuals who are supposedly equal to each other – husband and wife, for example. A hundred years ago, the husband was entitled to tell the wife what to do. There wasn’t much negotiation or flexibility. But when marriage becomes democratic, egalitarian, it’s a constant negotiation: The question of who does what is there all the time. The individual needs to clarify to himself what he wants and how he will avoid conflict. The stricter the prohibition on wielding authority, the stronger psychology becomes. It’s a very effective system in that it helps with comporting oneself in the world of negotiations. That’s one thing.”

Meaning there is a second thing.

“The second thing is that, if there is one element that characterizes modern life, it’s the fact that my identity is not a given – it too is in negotiation. Sociologists call it a society of evaluation. I need to choose a job, what to study, a place to live, a partner; life is a series of choices of which only the subject is the source. Today the individual is in an almost constant state of uncertainty.”

Uncertainty gives rise to anxiety.

Illouz's desire to connect ‘private happiness’ to ‘the general good’ – and, to be precise: to sever the possibility that an egoistical person will feel great and terrific and perfect – recalls many religious concepts in which the idea that 'the wicked thrive' is a dissonance that merits an outcry and demands an explanation from God.

“Uncertainty doesn’t square with people’s basic wiring. In a situation where people have to clarify nonstop who they are and what they want and what the right choice is, the psychologists are there as a tool of clarification and of honing and shaping identity.”

But your complaint about psychology doesn’t stop with the honing of identity.

“People have good reasons to go into therapy, because the conditions of modern existence are complex and also uncertain. But psychology tends to entrench your identity. What happened is that psychology from one side and identity politics from the other side have created an individual who is obsessive about their identity – be it sexual, gender, religious or ethnic.”

How does having an entrenched identity help one to conduct negotiations with the other in order to resolve conflicts?

“Ironically, people who have undergone therapy often develop boundaries that are less flexible. Effectively, the individual is far less capable of negotiating with others because of these shaped boundaries of identity. Another key reason for psychology’s attractiveness is that our self-worth is constantly being tested – in admission to school, in being hired, on dates, when you use a dating app.”

Tinder.

“Tinder, exactly. We are being tested all the time. You are now testing me, and I you. Society is in a situation of perpetual testing, so socially, our self-worth is the most important thing. You can upgrade your worth, elevate it. The whole occupation with self-worth stems from the modern conditions of existence. The psychologists’ role is to rehabilitate that worth and to build it up, when so many institutions are undermining it.”

And then positive psychology goes a step further by not making do with raising self-worth, but also wants you to be happy.

“Let’s talk for a moment about one of Seligman’s ideas, called ‘post-traumatic growth.’ That means, I was raped or I killed someone in war or a friend of mine was killed in war – in any event, I’ve undergone a trauma. The imperative of positive psychology is not only to overcome the trauma, but also to grow from it and develop internal resilience.”

What’s bad about growing?

“One of the great things about psychoanalysis and psychology is their focus on mental suffering and the fact that they have demarcated a detailed map of personal disorders or mental processes that cause us to suffer. Seligman rejects completely the importance of suffering and its treatment. He declares that he needs to take from the psyche only the mechanisms that will encourage positive thought and will give a person a sense of empowerment.”

A “sense of capability.”

“And that capability is supposed to lead us to eliminating the ability of a traumatic event to shatter the psyche. So I hope I answered you.”

You answered, but…

“Another significant difference is that, politically, it’s a quantum leap. In general, from the beginning, psychology entered a great many realms in society: manager-employee relations in corporations, prisons and rehabilitation programs, schools. What didn’t happen was an entry into the economy in that form. Seligman succeeded in creating a sub-field, in the economy of happiness, which claims that misery should be measured in terms of money. Happiness brings in money, misery costs you money. That’s a quantum leap.”

But it could be that after 100 years during which psychology functioned as an industry that dealt with human suffering, a stage arrived in which the culture wanted to deal with human happiness.

“Look, I see that it’s… No, no, I’m trying to find the right wording.”

That happens occasionally, those pauses. At first they seem related to language issues. Illouz is compelled to toggle between a slightly broken Hebrew and French and English. Subsequently the impression changes. The stoppages are related also, and perhaps principally, to her desire to think about her answers before replying. Not infrequently, she also admits that she doesn’t have a good answer to a question or that she’s ambivalent about it. In a culture of television panel discussions in which replies are well-worn configurations of declarations of talking points, there’s something refreshing about the fact that it’s not an automatic response that’s sitting opposite you.

“All religions and a large part of Western philosophy share the conception that the psyche needs shaping and good education,” she continues. “There is no good society if you don’t have good citizens. And good citizens need to possess a worthy psyche. Because of that, in my view, the psychological discussion cannot be disconnected from a more general discussion about the virtues.”

Illouz’s recurring desire to connect “private happiness” to “the general good” – and, to be precise: to sever the possibility that an egoistical person will feel great and terrific and perfect – recalls many religious concepts in which the idea that “the wicked thrive” is a dissonance that merits an outcry and demands an explanation from God. When Illouz insists on the affinity between “happiness” and the values of justice, equality and compassion, one can only imagine millions of patients in psychologists’ clinics recalling in a broken voice their early childhood and the formative moment when their mother forgot to pick them up from kindergarten, when suddenly Illouz enters the frame dramatically and scolds them: You really have had a hard time, but that doesn’t give you the license to behave in life as though only you exist!

“You asked why in effect this notion of happiness should be rejected. I don’t want to reject it. We do not want unhappy people. But I also think that there are parts of the psyche that Spinoza called ‘sad passions,’ such as envy or melancholy, and on the other hand, good passions such as happiness. We are living in an almost anti-Spinozist era, in which everything that exists I can and am even duty-bound to change. With Spinoza, one needs the ability to understand that these sad passions are a result of inflation of the self or of an incorrect understanding of the world. That too is a program of happiness, but very different.”

It’s definitely different from Coca-Cola’s happiness workshops, or from Facebook, and also from Google, which takes pride in its ultra-cool employment conditions. So they tell you, bring the dog with you to work, play ping-pong, go home when you feel like it, and on top of all that, you’ll get a high salary. Sounds good, all in all.

“The problem is that the workplace tries to shape the employees’ entire subjectivity. Modernity was based on a sharp separation between work, on the one hand, and family and the private sphere; and the more they were separated, the better it was for both spheres. What we have been witnessing in the past 30 years is that the employee has come to be identified according to such vague qualities as creativity and personality. The workplace increasingly assumed the authority to shape the employees’ emotional structure from within, and to demand in return a particular emotional structure, largely positive. That means that the workplace is fusing more and more with the private sphere.”

But maybe people would like to see that fusion happen. Maybe people are no longer interested in separation.

“Look, I’m not sure I have a good answer to that. I will say this: When there are two sides in the interaction, my sociological tendency is to say that if there is one side that is dramatically stronger, the interaction takes place, apparently because it serves that side. But I also agree with you: There is truly an exchange eros.”

Meaning?

“I think that the workplace has become the new eros. Many people’s identity is fulfilled by their work. In a certain sense, the workplace is more rewarding, more organized, the rules are clearer, there are multiple interactions. It’s a more aesthetic world: I dress well, I put on makeup, that means more stimuli. The workplace is becoming increasingly people’s central source of identity. What does that mean in practice? Because, to say ‘work’ sounds like a fine word, but it means that Facebook becomes your source of identity. Meaning that Google, or Coca-Cola, becomes our source of identity.”

People drive a company car with a sticker that says, “How am I driving?” The company car and I are one.

“I use buses and taxis.”

The plasticity of personality

Illouz (60, three children, not married) was born in Morocco, grew up in France, obtained a doctorate in the United States (communications and cultural studies, University of Pennsylvania), and arrived in Israel in 1991. She’s considered a sociological success story: She received a prize from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem for outstanding research, was awarded the title of Knight of the Legion of Honor in France in 2019, was invited to deliver the Adorno lecture series in Germany, holds the Rose Isaac Chair in Sociology at the Hebrew University, is a senior research fellow at both the Van Leer Jerusalem Institute and Hebrew University’s Center for the Study of Rationality, and is the author of 13 books (and is currently completing two more).

Nevertheless, at one point, I wondered whether Illouz wasn’t somewhat trapped in a fixed structure. She takes from humanity the great emotions – love, suffering, and now happiness – exposing them as synthetic products of the period and tracing the capitalist wiring that leads to all those “vested interests.”

The moment you say, at the collective level, that most of the bad things that happen are due to the fact that people don’t manage themselves well, you are effectively neutralizing political thought. That’s a danger to democracy.Illouz

“Because politics in my eyes is to give a name to suffering and to connect between my suffering, or say, that of my neighbor, and something that a different group is doing that is causing that suffering,” she says. “The political sphere must be able to allow for the possibility of building groups and even alliances between groups with very different identities. Suffering has undergone a process of privatization. Think of Betty Friedan, for example. When she wrote ‘The Feminine Mystique,’ she described a ‘problem that has no name,’ that’s what she called it. Namely, a situation in which women in those pseudo-American houses feel a great discomfort and blame themselves. She triggered a huge explosion, because she gave it a name – the situation of the woman within society. Our place is not in the house, polishing the floor and waiting for husband to come home. There is always a struggle between forces that want to privatize suffering and forces that are trying to give it a name, and not only to give it a name, but to point to a broad group that feels the same way.”

To translate this into Israeli: Think of the Israeli high-tech person who, though living in an “accessorized” mansion and having already booked a summer vacation in the Seychelles, also, on the other hand, works 12 hours a day, travels two hours in traffic jams, does too much reserve duty and pays too much in taxes. When that person feels pressure that turns into quarrels with his partner or into aggressive behavior in the public space, is the answer that “it’s all in the head” legitimate? Is therapy that analyzes “his place” in the family during very early childhood, or a quarter-hour of daily mindfulness meditation, really the solution?

“Everyone who is engaged in the pursuit of happiness thinks that the reason they’re not succeeding is their parents, who didn’t give them enough attention, who didn’t encourage them enough, or sometimes over-encouraged them,” Illouz says. “In other words, everything and its opposite becomes a source for explaining why I’m not succeeding, whereas in fact, the modern conditions of existence create wellbeing for only a small group. That is why I say that the language of psychology is changing the grounds of reality. The reality we live and feel today is the inner reality of what others have done to us and [the question of] how I can correct that from within myself. Politics requires a type of suspension of a conception like that, a type of detachment from the inner self, it calls for looking outward and identifying a common object. It is the identification of that common object that is missing so badly.”

So instead of politics we get coaching: Do it this way, do it differently.

“Yes, you have a task and now I am helping you solve a specific problem. Like positive psychology, coaching also believes in the plasticity of the personality, in the ability to change – both offer techniques for how to change. It’s you with yourself and with practices you need to do.”

A gym.

“Exactly.”

Maybe that’s why it’s called “coaching.”

“Yes, it’s a different conceptualization of the psyche.”

But maybe people are fed up with all the talk about mother, father, childhood? Maybe it shows that people are tired of classic psychology? “Just tell me what to do” – and that’s it.

“I would say that it perhaps shows that psychology, like coaching, also provides techniques for self-management. There is sometimes a very lovely and very impressive theoretical envelope in psychology, but when you discard that whole envelope, you are actually encouraging people to move away from their habits and patterns.”

Now she talks about the American historian T.J. Jackson Lears (“He’s fantastic”), whose 1981 book “No Place of Grace” (“a work of genius”) deals with the process in which, according to Illouz, “a person’s whole external envelope changes completely. Suddenly you see a lot of light in the streets, glowing billboards, films, media, luxurious display windows. The external façade of the world changes and changes the conception of reality. Since then we have entered a new phase, the virtual world, which renders even more extreme the feeling of loss of the concreteness of reality, so that the movement inward of people is also increasing. A feeling is being created that the external world, which is so saturated with images and pictures, is not real.”

One of the most common expressions of this today is the phrase “you’re not real.”

“Yes. And when the external world becomes so acutely disjointed, what really remains to you? You.”

And if so, it’s important for that “you” to be as authentic as possible.

“Authenticity is one of the most productive concepts, in the sense that it generates multiple economic and mental practicalities. For example, at the economic level, the ‘authentic product’ will always cost more. Authenticity is a marketing strategy that increases significantly the value of the product. Think of ‘authentic [ethnic] restaurants.’”

But what does it mean in the case of a person who wants to feel authentic?

“I’m ambivalent here, because the concept of authenticity expresses moral advancement. Take Ibsen’s plays, for example: They are filled with female characters who lead false lives from which they are trying to liberate themselves. Or Anna Karenina, for example. Obviously we all admire the fact that she is trying to live her life differently from the formulas and conventions her husband obeys. On the other hand, authenticity has become like Achilles in Zeno’s paradox.”

You’re constantly moving toward the goal, but you’ll never reach it.

“Abraham Maslow [the American psychologist who developed the humanistic approach] was among the first who put forward the idea of self-actualization. That notion draws a division between the life I have now and the life I could have, if only I knew how to eliminate all the inner barriers that are stopping me. Maslow also sees self-actualization as social success. How will I know that I have achieved self-actualization? By the fact that I will reach the summit. But there can be very few people at the summit, so it follows that not everyone can achieve self-actualization. Accordingly, that idea is false. The consumer culture, from the torn jeans to organic vegetables and a vacation in nature, also promises to connect us to a hidden part of ourselves.”

When people say they want to be authentic, what they mean is that they want to behave outwardly the way they feel and think inwardly.

“I think that what’s termed authentic is a variety of things. For example, if you are in a mediocre marriage, it’s very easy for you to say, ‘I am not living my authentic love in my marriage.’ What the British psychoanalyst Adam Phillips calls ‘the unlived life.’ We live in the shadow of the life we could be living.”

And in practice what happens?

“Our life is filled with contradictions and is bland.”

Or maybe it’s what we don’t want to accept at the underlying base: day-to-day life when it is not filled with thrills and achievements.

“So at this point I want to bring in the concept of the aestheticization of life. Aestheticization constitutes an important dimension of modern life; for example, media, movies and commercials present a beautiful body, characters that live in literary genres are defined well, with a beginning, a middle and an end. What does ‘aesthetic’ mean? It means that we imagine our life as though it were a novel. An aesthetic dimension is one in which everything has a place and has meaning. Now, life is not aesthetic. It contains an amorphous sequence of events that don’t exactly have logic between them. There is a very wide gulf between the aesthetic consciousness of life, which is constantly growing, and the ability to live a bland life that is filled with constraints.”

You see that aestheticization in Instagram, where everything is beautiful, everything is delightful.

“And all that is part of your self-staging. And happiness, I’m afraid, is also part of the same self-staging.

Entering the happiness machine

'Aesthetic' means that we imagine our life as though it were a novel. An aesthetic dimension is one in which everything has a place and has meaning. Now, life is not aesthetic.Illouz

The book about happiness sprang from an article Illouz published in Le Monde about the concept of “resilience.” “Seligman began to study depression in the 1970s,” she relates. “He conducted experiments in which electric shocks were administered to a group of dogs. One group could press a button and put a stop to the shocks, a second group could not. A few weeks later, the first group was able to reprise the experiment, while the second group wasn’t able to [stop the electric shocks].”

Sounds logical.

“But Seligman coined a very interesting phrase in the wake of the experiment: ‘learned helplessness.’ The group that learned that it was impossible to stop the suffering, stopped trying to overcome it. That could have been a fantastic psychological finding on the way to understanding why people give up in advance. But Seligman didn’t take it to that place. He conducted similar experiments on humans, using noise instead of electric shocks. He noticed that in the group that was supposed to learn helplessness, there were a few individuals who nevertheless managed to turn off the terrible noise. He took an interest in them, in the exceptions.”

They are the source of positive psychology.

“Exactly – that’s where it originated. And then he asks himself why these people are actually insisting on trying. And there he coins the term ‘resilience.’ Meaning that no matter what happens to people, how unlucky they may be, they are stubborn. And from that group he drew an inference for the rest.”

But what are the traits of the people who didn’t despair? Maybe they are simply obsessive people who repeat the same action over and over, with no connection to what’s happening around them? Because there is actually something natural about concluding, after you have tried and tried unsuccessfully, that it’s apparently impossible.

“What you are saying in a different way is also something that [I am] saying. Namely, that possibly what these positive traits indicate is the existence of a type of impenetrability: that a resilient person is effectively an impenetrable person. And there is no way you can distinguish between the good, positive person and the impenetrable, disconnected person.”

So maybe the world is developing emotional detachment as some sort of mass mechanism that will make it possible to survive modern life?

“Yes, I think that there is such a tendency and such a danger. The problem with the concept of emotional resilience is that you aren’t able to distinguish between mental resilience and disconnect.”

But maybe that disconnect has advantages. Maybe those who can disconnect themselves from negative feelings and remain only with the positive ones prefers this option?

“Robert Nozick [the late American philosopher] conducted an interesting conceptual exercise. He said we should imagine a happiness machine that people are put into, and in which they can imagine whatever they want for 80 years. For example, that you’re married to the most beautiful woman and that you are the most successful author and that your life is wonderful. Nozick wanted to say, ‘Well, obviously we won’t choose a life like that,’ we would prefer a real, less happy, life. But it seems to me that at the collective level we are less and less capable of making the distinction and of wanting to make the distinction, between an imagined life and imagined happiness and the real thing.”

Meaning that there are people who would say, “Let’s do it – put me in the happiness machine.”

“I think there will be more like that. In other words, possibly we have exchanged intuition; it changed because of the virtual world. It’s like asking whether some who takes drugs and gets a good feeling from it, is doing something proper or improper. I don’t really have an answer, I would say that it depends on your philosophy of happiness. If you believe that there should be some sort of meaning that is outside you and outside the feelings you experience, and if you believe in the substantiality of the world, then it’s not enough.”

What do you mean by the “substantiality of the world”?

“For me it means that there is always something that is resisting my desire or my wish. That is the substantiality of the world.”

Does there always have to be something that’s resisting me?

“It means that you are born into a world that cannot fulfill all your needs, your desires. This occupies a central place throughout Lacan’s psychoanalysis. He says that lack is the essence of desire, it is absolutely part of humanity’s being. So the question is whether you think it’s possible to live without that lack. I think not.”

Positive psychology tries to erase that lack.

“Completely. It doesn’t try – it erases it. It rejects it out of hand.”

But after all the brainwashing, is it really possible to erase that lack in people?

“You can deny the existence of the lack very cleverly.”

So, when someone reports that they don’t feel the lack?

“The prevailing ideology of positiveness and happiness compels us not to report it.”

But some people do report it. Are they lying?

“Clearly there is a situation in which a person feels in harmony with their life. About people like that, as Tolstoy would note, I don’t have much to say. But one of Freud’s central concepts, and I very much believe in them, is denial. In the first stage, in the psyche, and afterward at the political level. So, in order to distinguish between a false report about happiness and an appropriate report, I would ask about the degree of effort involved in the experience of the denial.”

Please explain.

“If a person makes an effort to deny, invests mental energy, I would say that the happy identity there is imposed or borrowed, also at the collective level.”

On the broiling, empty porch of the cafeteria, with a spectacular view of the Old City spread out in front of us, Illouz mentions “a wonderful novel that I am wild about, ‘The Remains of the Day’ [by Kazuo Ishiguro]. There is the character of the butler, Mr. Stevens, who is totally characterized by the effort to deny. The narrator lets us understand what the character does not grasp. Now, the butler lives his life obediently, carrying out his role as a butler meticulously, apart from one small moment in which he understands the great lost opportunity of his life. But very quickly his consciousness shuts down again. For me, moments of disillusionment like that, despite the inherent pain, are worth more than happiness, or at least not less than it.”

So a moment before his consciousness shuts down, would you say to him, “Take note, you are living in denial,” or would you prefer to let him go insular and return to his previous life?

“In a letter that Freud wrote to his future wife, Martha, in 1883, he says: Let’s be truthful – psychoanalysis is only for people of means. He writes that the working class, if we were to open its consciousness, would not be able to profit from that consciousness, because its conditions of existence will remain very limited. Freud says that we don’t want to do that.”

So we don’t want to?

“It’s a text that amazed me when I read it. Because Freud effectively recognized that class differences exist and that opening the consciousness can be useful only for an established class; in other words, that a disillusioned consciousness is meaningful only if the conditions of existence allow something to be done with it. To open the consciousness of someone who doesn’t have anything to do with that consciousness is neither useful nor moral.”

So, do you tell him or not?

“It’s a dilemma.”
