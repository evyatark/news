The dramatic moment when Israel almost struck Iran

He was in the room at every critical, defense-related moment in the past decades. Now, Zohar Palti, who held senior Mossad and Defense Ministry posts, recalls what happened when Netanyahu and Barak ordered the army to prepare an Iran strike – within 16 days

Amos Harel

לחצו כאן

Ten minutes into the conversation with Zohar Palti, who this week concluded his term as head of the Defense Ministry’s political-military bureau, it was clear that he wasn’t about to go easy either on himself or on the system in which he had spent nearly 40 years. 

“One time, when I gave a talk in the Mossad’s senior command course, I was asked which past leader I would like to talk to if I could go back in time,” he relates. “I told the cadets: If only I could talk to Yitzhak Rabin, even for just five minutes, I would ask him one thing: Why in the world did you wait and not take us out of Lebanon? Why did we have to wait for more than 15 years, until Ehud Barak arrived on the scene? We were all doormat officers. None of us, from the rank of lieutenant and up, came out against the political echelon and their decision to remain in southern Lebanon. It was the silence of the lambs. Barak was right, of course. We should have gotten out long before that.”

And then Palti adds, almost in the same breath, “the fact that I stayed in the system for all those years afterward, and that I was a central part of the opposition to attacking Iran, is part of my process of atonement for Lebanon. It was compensation for our being captives to the dumb slogans that left us in Lebanon for no reason.”

Like his predecessor in the bureau, which represents the Defense Ministry on questions of strategic importance and vis-a-vis other states, Maj. Gen. (res.) Amos Gilad, Palti is one of the few people who were present at all the junctions at which critical security and state-policy decisions were made during the past few decades in Israel. Even before that, as a young intelligence officer, he served in the field in all the burning arenas in which Israel conducted bloody campaigns, in both Lebanon and the territories. He served as a brigade intelligence officer in the Israeli security zone in southern Lebanon, the intelligence officer of the Israel Defense Forces’ Judea and Samaria Division on the eve of the eruption of the second intifada in September 2000, and as head of the terrorism unit in the research division of Military Intelligence at the time of the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks and afterward. He began working in the Mossad around the time of the second Lebanon war, in 2006, serving there, successively, as head of the counterterrorism division, director of strategic research and head of the intelligence directorate.  

הרשמה מהירה לניוזלטר

הירשמו להמלצות הקריאה של הארץ

During his five years as head of the political-military bureau in the Defense Ministry, he served under no fewer than four ministers: Avigdor Lieberman (who appointed him to the post), Benjamin Netanyahu, Naftali Bennett and Benny Gantz. In these years he played a significant role in Israel’s struggle against the Iranian nuclear project and in behind-the-scenes confrontations over the formulation of Israeli policy on a variety of issues. In addition, Palti played a central role in managing Israel’s ties with friendly Sunni-Muslim countries: Jordan, Egypt, the United Arab Emirates and a few others that aren’t talked about publicly, even in the post-Abraham Accords era.

Palti, 58, a native of Ramat Hasharon, where he still lives, was drafted into the Intelligence Corps in November 1982, a few months after the start of the Lebanon War. In short order he found himself, as a noncom and afterward as a young officer, spending long periods in Lebanon, during which the Israel Defense Forces gradually pulled back to the Awali River line and afterward further south to the Litani River. 

“The [current] chief of staff, Aviv Kochavi, and I were in the same draft,” he relates. “Our generation in the army was shaped entirely in Lebanon. If I were to decide to undergo psychotherapy now, it would be in connection with that period. The sense of responsibility that was foisted on us as young officers was huge. That was the time, from the 1970s on, when terrorist squads infiltrated the Lebanon border and perpetrated attacks on Moshav Margaliot, Moshav Avivim and other places.

“There was this sign they put up in the operations room: ‘The mission – protecting the communities in the north.’ We took it with profound seriousness. If there were to be a penetration into a community in which children died, it would have been on our account. That’s a tremendous psychological burden for a guy of 21. I still get flashbacks from that period of patrol vehicles on the intrusion-tracking dirt road. You go to sleep wondering: Did I check that off the list or not?”

Another period immediately leaps to Palti’s mind. “Years later, in October 1992, a convoy of ours was hit by explosive devices near Kaukaba, in the eastern sector of the security zone. The soldiers traveled in armored Safari vehicles at the time, so Hezbollah hung fragmentation devices on electricity poles – which would enable them to strike high up, in the unarmored section of the truck. Five soldiers were killed. Kaplan [the nickname of Moshe Kaplinsky, later a major general and deputy chief of staff] was the commander of the territorial brigade, and the senior officer who arrived at the scene. He wanted to spare the young soldiers the grisly sight, so he moved the rest of the troops back and by himself evacuated the bodies of those who were killed in the truck.”

What Palti thinks today is that all that effort, with its multitudes of dead and wounded, was actually in vain – because the forces could have been moved south back across the border into Israel as early as the mid-1980s. “Four Mothers [a protest movement dedicated to bringing about an Israeli withdrawal from Lebanon] understood that before we did. Often there are civilian groups, whether left-wing or right-wing, that reveal other angles to you. You need to talk to them, listen to them, take into consideration that maybe they’re right. My decision to be part of the fortified wall that opposed an attack on nuclear sites in Iran in 2012 stems in part from the lessons I drew from the case of our remaining in Lebanon.”

This is the first time Palti has addressed publicly the dispute over whether to attack Iran. The usual focus is on the period of 2009-2011, when Benjamin Netanyahu as prime minister and Ehud Barak as defense minister pushed for an attack but encountered extensive opposition from top figures in the defense establishment. Palti was involved then, too, as head of the research division in the Mossad; in 2011, the agency’s new chief, Tamir Pardo, appointed him to the more senior position of head of the organization’s Intelligence Directorate, giving him a place near the head of the table. It was only in 2012, Palti says, that the internal debate in the Israeli security establishment reached a peak, “and someone will yet write books about those months.” 

“Ask those who were there,” he says. “I would start every intelligence survey presentation to the political decision makers by stating: The Iranians are not building a nuclear bomb. That drove Bibi and Ehud crazy. Why did I start by making that statement, which was based on solid information? Because if they’re not yet building a bomb, there is no urgency to attack.”

That assertion contradicted Barak’s theory of an Iranian “zone of immunity,” by which time the Iranians would have moved their means of production underground, thus safeguarding them against bombing and destruction. Barak believed it was urgent to attack before Iran reached that stage. Ten years later, he says, “one can judge who was right, Ehud or us. Cognitively, he’s the smartest person there is, which is exactly how he sees himself. To sit with him for hours is a real experience. He also has phenomenal rhetorical ability. On the other hand, I saw him and Netanyahu in 2012, and it was one of the most dangerous things that I’ve ever seen. We were in the room when it happened.”

Palti here confirms, in greater detail and with more color, accounts that have been mentioned since then by others who took part in the discussions, including Mossad heads Meir Dagan and Tamir Pardo, chiefs of staff Gabi Ashkenazi and Benny Gantz, and the Shin Bet security service director at the time, Yuval Diskin. This is testimony from within, from the inner sanctum, about one of the most dramatic chapters in Israel’s security history since the 1973 Yom Kippur War. Even though I have spoken about this with a number of those who were involved, and have read testimonies of others, I don’t think I have ever heard the matter voiced so bluntly.

“We would hold discussions in the ‘aquarium’ in the Prime Minister’s Office [the office set off by glass doors from the rest of the Prime Minister’s Office, in Jerusalem],” Palti relates, “and afterward we [security professionals] would go down to the parking area next to the bureau. Maj. Gen. Tal Russo, who was the head of the IDF’s Operations Directorate, would light a gas burner on the hood of his jeep and make black coffee. We stood there like that – Benny Gantz, Tamir, the IDF’s Planning Directorate head Amir Eshel, Military Intelligence head Kochavi and me – drinking coffee, looking at one another and asking: Hey, what just happened here? What do they want? Do they really intend to attack like that, out of the blue? 

“It’s 2012 that was the year of the major event, the real story,” he continues. “There’s no comparison between that and the first years of the dispute, with all their sound and fury. During the spring-summer of 2012, the operational preparations reached a peak. In my understanding, they [Netanyahu and Barak] were truly preparing to attack. I am not claiming to read the secrets of the heart. But when the IDF is instructed to deploy for ‘P+16’ [possible attack within 16 days], when air force aerial refueling planes are brought back from long-term servicing, it’s an emergency situation. The air force put all its planes into the air in order to prepare. Quite a few risks were taken in that period.”

Barak believed it was urgent to attack before Iran moved its nuclear production underground. Ten years later, Palti says, "one can judge who was right, Ehud or us … it was one of the most dangerous things that I’ve ever seen"

There are many people who are convinced to this day that they didn’t really intend to go ahead – that Netanyahu was incapable or ordering an attack and Barak didn’t want to, that it was all one big deception vis-à-vis the Americans and the world. 

“So they were just pulling the wool over the eyes of the chief of staff, the director of Military Intelligence, the commander of the air force? What did they gain from that? They carried out a deception operation at the expense of the chief of staff? I don’t buy that. To this day, there are arguments between the people involved about whether they really meant to [attack]. I’m certain that they did.”

Three people, according to Palti, lay themselves over the barbed-wire fence in order to express their objections to an attack: Pardo, Eshel and Palti himself. “Maybe I’m idealizing things a little in retrospect. But that was a red line for us. We all told them: If you issue an order, we will do everything to execute it.” But before that, they fought for their position. Apparently the strength of the opposition, almost wall to wall in the defense establishment hierarchy, was sufficient. Toward the fall of 2012, initial cracks appeared between Netanyahu and Barak. 

The defense minister gradually changed his mind, relying, among other sources, on a forecast by performance-analysis experts in the air force, who estimated that an Israeli attack on Iran would delay the latter’s nuclear project by a year and a half at most. Netanyahu effectively remained alone, in the face of American opposition and sweeping objections from the experts in Israel. The prime minister also did not have a majority who supported him in the “group of eight,” the select body of ministers that met frequently in order to deliberate the possibility of mounting an attack. 

In retrospect, some of Netanyahu’s supporters alleged that Meir Dagan, while in service, had deliberately betrayed secrets to the Americans in order to thwart the attack together with them.

“We did not go to tell the Americans. I think Dagan spoke to them with great openness, by virtue of his close relations with senior figures in the administration. But I don’t believe he gave them secret information that they didn’t know themselves,” notes Palti, who was very close both to Dagan and to his successor, Pardo. “What very much hastened the American progress toward an interim agreement with Iran, in 2013, and then the nuclear accord in 2015, was in part actually our behavior. We gave them a certain feeling of urgency [because of the threats of an attack], and that is what prompted the Obama administration to create a secret back channel of negotiations with Iran. A year later the interim agreement was signed, and the idea of an attack was dropped.”

Why, then, in contrast, had it been necessary to attack the reactor in Syria, in 2007?

“A significant difference exists between the threat that was posed by the Iranian nuclear project and the threat from the Syrian nuclear facility. In 2007, there was no real dilemma. From the moment the information arrived that the reactor in Syria would become hot [active and radioactive] within a few months, the [Ehud] Olmert government had no choice but to attack. That was also our recommendation – the IDF and the Mossad. And keep in mind the conditions under which we arrived at that decision. The IDF was still recovering from the 2006 war in Lebanon, Ehud Olmert was immersed in [criminal] investigations. And despite all that, we went for it despite the risk that a third Lebanon war would erupt. 

“There were no real questions, because it was obvious that it was essential. But what are you going to attack in Iran? An enrichment facility? I am in favor of attacking if there is an urgent need, if a sword is held to your throat, as Dagan put it. But that was not the situation in 2012 or in the preceding years.”

But what if you’re wrong and Netanyahu is right, and the right thing to do is to wage a fierce battle, including against the new nuclear accord that is now emerging?

“Similar arguments were made against me in 2012, too. I take into account that I might be wrong, and I ask myself that question almost every day. But let’s examine what happened between 2009 and 2012, the years in which the political decision makers wanted to attack the nuclear sites from the air. Did Iran acquire a bomb during those years? According to the foreign media, some of the delays in their program are related to things that Israel, and also other countries, apparently did in secret. Destructive malware, mysterious bombs, equipment breakdowns. There are things that can and should be done, other than an air attack.”

What will be the effect of the new agreement, which is apparently about to be signed in Vienna?

“From Israel’s point of view, both the previous agreement, and even more so the new one, are, in simple Hebrew, shitty. The new agreement doesn’t address all of our basic concerns, about the issues left unresolved [following the 2015 accord], the suspicions of previous Iranian violations, down to the nature of the supervision over the centrifuges. On the other hand, no one is actually asking us. In the eyes of the United States and the Europeans, what is important now is to stop the enrichment of uranium, before the Iranians move up from a 60-percent enrichment level to 90 percent. And they also want to remove the Iranian threat from the agenda and focus on what is urgent for them at the moment: China and Russia-Ukraine. 

“From the moment the agreement is signed, billions of dollars will flow into Iran. That money will not reach the civilian population, it will go first of all toward financing the terrorism of the Revolutionary Guards. The Americans need to understand that in the end they too will be dragged into having to contend with Iran again, whether because of Iranian terrorism or because clauses of the agreement will expire and policy will have to be formulated in the face of renewed progress in the nuclear project.”

‘I don’t want to see this’

Even though Palti is already talking as someone from whom a heavy weight of authority and responsibility has been removed, he does not use the opportunity of an interview to undertake a direct settling of accounts with the civilian policy makers. Apart from his description of the specific disagreement over the Iranian affair, he displays great esteem toward those in charge whom he worked with. And despite the differences of approach between right and left, he adds, one can discern without difficulty a national security conception that was accepted by all the prime ministers he worked under.

Our generation in the army was shaped entirely in Lebanon. If I were to decide to undergo psychotherapy now, it would be in that context. The sense of responsibility that was foisted on us as young officers was huge.Palti

“In 2002, I was head of the terrorism arena in Military Intelligence, at age 38. We brought the intelligence and the operational plan, which four years later would be translated into Operation Density in Lebanon, in which the IDF destroyed Hezbollah’s Fajr [long-range] missiles at the start of the war. I was sent to the prime minister, Ariel Sharon, to brief him in a meeting held in Ben-Gurion House [in Tel Aviv]. I presented everything to him in 45 minutes. He took everything down in a notebook, said to me, ‘Thank you very much, young colonel,’ turned to his military secretary, Kaplinsky, and said to him: I don’t want to see this in the near future. All the way back to the office in the Kirya [defense establishment headquarters in Tel Aviv], I thought: Why doesn’t he want to see it?

“Two years earlier, when Barak was still prime minister, Hezbollah abducted three soldiers on Har Dov [a mountain on the Israel-Lebanon border]. Barak had promised that if Hezbollah only tried to do something, the earth in Lebanon would tremble. But this time it was decided almost not to respond. Why, in both cases, did Barak and Sharon not take immediate action? My explanation is that they both thought and acted like military men who know how to manage risks. In the army there is a main effort and a secondary effort. Barak had just run into the intifada, Sharon was preparing for Operation Defensive Shield in the territories. They focused on what they saw as the main thing.

“Prime ministers in this country have to work vis-a-vis several clocks, which aren’t always synchronized. The entire Israeli public falls under their responsibility, but at the same time, they have to manage the country’s relations with the Americans, and also take world public opinion into consideration. Yes, including the Europeans – 30 percent of our trade takes place with Europe. There are leaderships in Germany, Britain, France that basically have our well-being good at heart. And there is also the question of the IDF’s preparedness: the condition of the inventory, the munitions, the level of training of the units. Those are not data that are available to the public, but prime ministers have to take them into account when making critical decisions. There are almost no political considerations in those decisions by prime ministers. 

“They must take into consideration urgent security problems. Nonetheless, I think a military background for a prime minister is absolutely not critical. Thinking like that is mistaken in a democracy. There is a strong professional system here that will readily support every leader, to help in making the best decisions possible. I was more favorably impressed by some of those with a civilian background than by the security people [who served as prime minister].”

In the IDF, and then in the Mossad, Palti worked alongside some of the most senior figures in the Israeli defense establishment of the past few decades. Among those who influenced him along the way, he mentions Maj. Gens. Kaplinsky and Matan Vilnai (Palti was the latter’s bureau chief as deputy chief of staff) and Mossad chiefs Dagan and Pardo. 

“Matan taught me how to plan on a large scale. From Kaplan I learned how to treat people under my command. With Dagan I had a deep, inexplicable connection. He recruited me to the Mossad with one phone call, at 11:30 at night. It was impossible to say no to him. The state owes to him and to Tamir the success of the operation to destroy the Syrian reactor. I remember a discussion in which Pardo raised an objection to a suggestion that came up, and people didn’t take in what he wanted. Ehud Barak stopped the discussion and said: ‘I want you to listen to Tamir. Having commanded certain operational units in the Mossad, he has experience that none of the rest of us have.’ In general, I think that pluralism is a very strong characteristic of the Israeli system. That was so with Dagan and Pardo, also with Barak and Netanyahu, and also with the defense ministers I worked with: Lieberman, Bennett and Gantz. 

“The pluralism of opinions in the room is one of the system’s great assets. In the end, in major issues, state considerations are decisive. In the dispute over Iran in 2012, too, it’s not that we won and they [Netanyahu and Barak] lost. I am pleased that they did not make the decision to attack, but if they had done so, we would have executed it.”

‘Amazing achievement’

Palti, who for years played a central role in Israel’s secret ties with the Gulf states, sees the Abraham Accords, which Netanyahu signed in the fall of 2020, establishing diplomatic relations with both the United Arab Emirates and Bahrain, as an “amazing achievement. People in Israel don’t understand it sufficiently. I am still apprehensive that we will screw it up. A national project coordinator needs to be assigned to [cultivate] those relations, one who will look at the whole picture and synchronize the activity of government ministries. There are allegations that the Defense Ministry is supposedly dragging its feet in authorizing security transactions with the UAE and Bahrain. I think it’s better that way. I didn’t think it was necessary to rush to be first in the security realm. The other side didn’t want that, either. They wanted to establish civilian contracts in the economic, high-tech, desalination realms. That’s what rivets them. 

“We are building agreements here for years, and peace is established through relations between people. That is the lesson of the peace agreements with Egypt and Jordan, which were not translated into ties between the peoples. The masses of Israeli tourists in Dubai – that’s what’s important in my eyes. The security industries in Israel are complaining that we lost five months [after the agreements were signed], but in my opinion it was necessary so that there will be order in the system. Now things have started to move. The government bears tremendous responsibility, to move ahead and develop the relations with them and to preserve the Abraham Accords. That is the future. I hope we won’t wake up in another few years and say that we blew those relations. 

“Our interlocutors in the Gulf measure us by several criteria. They admire Israeli technology and the achievements of the security industries. The Arrow missiles, Iron Dome, satellites – those are the brands everyone wants to have. The images of civilians in restaurants with missiles being fired around them, the photograph that looks like ‘Star Wars’ – when Iron Dome intercepts volleys above Ashkelon – impressed them very much. But contrary to the reports, we have not sold Iron Dome batteries to any country. We gave one battery to the Americans, but they didn’t transfer it to other countries.”

He notes that “our closeness to the United States is a huge lever of influence, a tremendous asset in the face of the Arab states. Many times I sat in closed rooms [with Arab officials], and messages to the Americans were transferred through me. America is the most significant foundation in our national security.”

Palti’s esteem for the Americans is evident throughout my conversations with him. He has professional ties of many years with senior figures in successive administrations, from the Defense Department and the State Department to the various intelligence agencies. Last week, during a farewell visit to the Pentagon, he was awarded the Department of Defense Medal for Distinguished Public Service, one of the most prestigious honors the administration can award to foreign nationals. The medal, said Under Secretary of Defense Colin Kahl, was given to Palti for “his significant contribution to strengthening the strategic security ties between the two countries.”

“There is no substitute for the United States,” Palti says. “Anyone in Israel who is toying with the idea of moving away from American influence is making a fundamental mistake. In my last job, I worked with two administrations, Republican and Democrat, and both saw equally to ensuring Israel’s qualitative edge. We have no friend like them in the world. From the F-35 to the M-16, almost all the weapons we acquire are manufactured by them and purchased with American security aid funds. The White House and the majority of Congress are on our side. As is public opinion. That is based on values the two countries share: democracy, human dignity, freedom of expression. We must preserve that for all we’re worth. I am also hearing other voices, in the progressive camp, and I find that disturbing. We are blessed to have them on our side, and we must not take it for granted. We should always be on the side of the Americans. They are a nation that develops vaccines, is looking for medications for cancer, sends astronauts into space – overall, they are looking out for the good of humanity.”

That identification need not be translated into a strategic defense pact with the United States, in his view, along the lines that Netanyahu considered – whether as a gimmick or with serious intent – amid the recent spate of elections. “The possibility of our relying on ourselves is an acute security need. It’s not by chance that we refrained from [entering into] a defense pact all these years. You don’t want someone telling you: Don’t attack in Syria now, or for Congress to prevent you from attacking because you are liable to endanger the lives of American soldiers in the future.”

Palti demurs, cautiously, from comments voiced in Netanyahu’s circle in his last years in office to the effect that Israel can rely only on the support of Orthodox Jews and the Christian evangelicals in the United States, and that it is impossible to count on the support of Reform Jewry. “We cannot forgo any Jew anywhere. I don’t accept the claim that we lost them long ago. We have to fight for the bond with each of them. The crisis with American Jewry over the Western Wall arrangements is in my view a strategic issue for Israel’s national security. It has to be resolved.”

Another source of strength for Israel, Palti says, stems from the fact that the neighboring countries see Israelis as people who dare, who don’t think twice when an offensive action is necessary. “From their point of view, there is a certain aura about us, which emanates from the destruction of the Iraqi reactor in 1981 and the Syrian reactor in 2007. They also took the same attitude toward the preparations for attacking Iran and for the attacks in the ‘campaign between the wars,’ which is now being waged in Syria. They appreciate our ability to quarrel with the U.S. administration when necessary. Netanyahu made a mistake, in my view, when he addressed Congress against the nuclear agreement in 2015, but that move won him a lot of points in the Arab states.”

Where we were wrong

The solution will be confined to Judea and Samaria. With Hamas there’s nothing to discuss. They don’t recognize us and can’t be part of the solution if they don’t revise that stance.Palti

Anyone who seeks to locate Palti on the left side of the political map, given his opposition to attacking Iran and his view of the essential character of relations with the Sunni Arab countries, may be surprised to hear his views on the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. “The Oslo process died long ago,” he says. “That is not a political position, it’s my professional opinion. There were two main reasons for that. The first is the loss of mutual trust caused by the second intifada, with the suicide attacks – damage that will be very difficult to repair. The second is Hamas’ takeover of the Gaza Strip in 2007, two years after the ‘disengagement.’ 

“It’s impossible now to talk about the realization of the two-state vision,” he continues, “because there are actually three entities here, and that’s how things are unfolding. The Gaza Strip is a completely separate entity, and the main thing that’s preventing union between the West Bank and Gaza is the harsh internal rivalry between the Palestinian Authority and Hamas.

“Does that mean that the two-state solution should not remain part of the public debate? No. But in any event, the solution will be confined to Judea and Samaria. With Hamas there is nothing to talk about regarding a true political process. That organization does not recognize us and can’t be part of the solution as long as it is does not revise its basic stance. Our strategy now rests largely on the understanding that at this stage the situation is not ripe for a settlement that will include concrete concessions, not from the Palestinian point of view and not for most citizens of Israel. There is no possibility at the moment of furthering the two-state solution. 

“Much has changed on the ground: the roads, the demography. It’s impossible to cling to the past. We will need to adjust the discussion to the reality that’s going to exist on the ground for another few years. We are in a status quo [situation] until the Israeli public will decide otherwise. It’s clear to me that a single state will be a disaster. The Palestinians are talking about that a lot lately, and that talk worries me.”

Since the Israeli pullout from Gaza [in 2005], Israel has launched four military operations in the Strip and almost nothing has changed. Where did we go wrong?

“Our dilemma in Gaza is [deciding] between continuation of the existing policy and a broader opening of the Strip – perhaps not as far as Shimon Peres’ Singapore vision. Billions of dollars from the international community have flowed into Gaza. At the moment, Hamas is not sufficiently looking after the residents there. More money is being invested in building up its military force than in water, sewerage and electricity infrastructure. It’s not Israel that delayed the establishment of a desalination plant in the Gaza Strip; Hamas is not ensuring that the children there have normal water to drink.”

Our conflict with Gaza won’t be over soon, Palti says, citing his own family’s story as proof. “In the ‘50s, my father, Uri, was a member of the Nahal group that founded Kibbutz Nahal Oz, on the Gaza border. He had a friend, Roi Rotberg, who was the security coordinator of the kibbutz. One morning, Rotberg went out on patrol along the furrow that marked the border. Palestinians kidnapped him and killed him. His horse came back to the kibbutz on her own, and Roi’s body was returned only the next day, via UN personnel, after being subjected to serious abuse. The chief of staff, Moshe Dayan, made his famous eulogy over Roi’s grave. A decade later, my father took part in the conquest of Gaza during the Six-Day War. Two decades later, I served as an intelligence officer in Golani during the First Intifada. And now, more than 30 years later, my son is an intelligence officer in the Gaza division.” 

Palti sounds far more optimistic when he talks about the ties with Jordan and Egypt, two countries he has visited dozens of times over the years, albeit far from the media’s eye. “Relations with both are among the greatest strategic assets we possess,” he says. “We have survived so many bumps in the road with them. There is hardly any sort of crisis we haven’t gone through with them. The rise to power of President Abdel-Fatah al-Sissi upgraded all the relations with Egypt, after difficult years.”

In 2012, in the midst of Operation Pillar of Defense, in Gaza, Netanyahu sent Palti and Mossad head Pardo to Cairo, to wrap up a cease-fire agreement with Hamas with the help of Egypt. The Egyptian government was then being led, for a short time, by the Muslim Brotherhood, following the toppling of the regime of Hosni Mubarak. 

Palti: “Listen, to drive through the streets of Cairo in an unarmored Peugeot 406 under the rule of the Muslim Brotherhood is a formative experience. You’re used to visiting there with a secure convoy, but at that time they were afraid to show that they were in contact with us. Things improved immediately when Sissi came to power. 

“The Americans made a lot of mistakes regarding Egypt, but in the end the triangular relationship between them and us and Egypt is critical. It must be preserved no matter what. Regarding Jordan, I am familiar with all the complaints against them, but what they give us is of tremendous value. In the end, that is our longest border, and the IDF barely even needs to invest in guarding it. The Jordanians see to that and provide us with strategic depth.”

In the past month, Haaretz quoted senior sources in the defense establishment who urged reconsidering the possibility of an offensive campaign against Hezbollah, given the apprehension that the Shi’ite organization has vastly improved the accuracy of its arsenal of rockets in Lebanon. Palti read those remarks and is against the idea. “I suggest to the whole generation who knew not Lebanon to take a deep breath and show restraint. You don’t mess with Lebanon if there’s no need. Only if there is necessity and a threat will we need to act,” he says.

“In Lebanon, the situation is one of ‘zero or one hundred.’ If you get into a war there, you will get to the point where you are destroying cities. Therefore, we suggest to the government of Lebanon to prevent that process, not to get to that point. But when there are recommendations from the IDF to attack in Lebanon like in Syria, in a campaign between the wars, I find that to be a bad strategic idea. We have no real border dispute with Lebanon, a compromise can be found for the gas reserves in the Mediterranean, and the dispute over the exact place where the land border will run is resolvable. On the other hand, the potential threat from there is immense. As long as they don’t strike at our civilians, there are no grounds for a war against them. That is something we learned over the years: You go to war only when there is no choice. That is a central element of our security conception.”

Between the two conversations I held with Palti, Russia invaded Ukraine and Europe found itself in the most serious security crisis since the collapse of the Soviet bloc at the beginning of the 1990s. Palti urges Israeli caution on this issue and supports the line taken by Prime Minister Bennett. “We are doing a great deal to assist in the humanitarian sphere. Some are trying to drag us into direct involvement in the confrontation itself, but Israel doesn’t have to occupy a place at the center of the stage in every conflict. Forces far larger than us are involved here: Russia, the United States, the countries of Europe, NATO. I am appalled when I see the heartbreaking sights from the war, but I don’t think that our taking a side will turn things around. Everything that is happening there will have immediate implications for the situation here, and it is impossible to ignore the fact that lately we have a big neighbor [Russia] on our northern border.”

One lesson for Israel, he avers, will be to preserve its technological military advantage. “We see now what is happening in Ukraine, when they are compelled to mobilize weapons systems from here and from there, and learn how to use them. An army needs years to absorb advanced weapons systems. In light of the threats, Israel must constantly be on the cutting edge of technology, both in the development of missile-intercept capabilities, including lasers, and also with offensive ability.”

This will obligate an increase in the Israeli defense budget, he says, as will also be the case in many European countries, which can be expected to make large-scale purchases from the Israeli military industries. “The economy here is quite thriving because the security situation is relatively good,” he observes. “Routine security activity and the larger operations are very costly, but in the end they also boost the economy. In this environment, the security activity ensures you a level of quiet and stability. People have short memories. They tend to forget what happened here during the second intifada and how that affected the economy.

נרשמת בהצלחה!

Ten minutes into the conversation with Zohar Palti, who this week concluded his term as head of the Defense Ministry’s political-military bureau, it was clear that he wasn’t about to go easy either on himself or on the system in which he had spent nearly 40 years. 

“One time, when I gave a talk in the Mossad’s senior command course, I was asked which past leader I would like to talk to if I could go back in time,” he relates. “I told the cadets: If only I could talk to Yitzhak Rabin, even for just five minutes, I would ask him one thing: Why in the world did you wait and not take us out of Lebanon? Why did we have to wait for more than 15 years, until Ehud Barak arrived on the scene? We were all doormat officers. None of us, from the rank of lieutenant and up, came out against the political echelon and their decision to remain in southern Lebanon. It was the silence of the lambs. Barak was right, of course. We should have gotten out long before that.”

And then Palti adds, almost in the same breath, “the fact that I stayed in the system for all those years afterward, and that I was a central part of the opposition to attacking Iran, is part of my process of atonement for Lebanon. It was compensation for our being captives to the dumb slogans that left us in Lebanon for no reason.”

Like his predecessor in the bureau, which represents the Defense Ministry on questions of strategic importance and vis-a-vis other states, Maj. Gen. (res.) Amos Gilad, Palti is one of the few people who were present at all the junctions at which critical security and state-policy decisions were made during the past few decades in Israel. Even before that, as a young intelligence officer, he served in the field in all the burning arenas in which Israel conducted bloody campaigns, in both Lebanon and the territories. He served as a brigade intelligence officer in the Israeli security zone in southern Lebanon, the intelligence officer of the Israel Defense Forces’ Judea and Samaria Division on the eve of the eruption of the second intifada in September 2000, and as head of the terrorism unit in the research division of Military Intelligence at the time of the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks and afterward. He began working in the Mossad around the time of the second Lebanon war, in 2006, serving there, successively, as head of the counterterrorism division, director of strategic research and head of the intelligence directorate.  

הרשמה מהירה לניוזלטר

הירשמו להמלצות הקריאה של הארץ

During his five years as head of the political-military bureau in the Defense Ministry, he served under no fewer than four ministers: Avigdor Lieberman (who appointed him to the post), Benjamin Netanyahu, Naftali Bennett and Benny Gantz. In these years he played a significant role in Israel’s struggle against the Iranian nuclear project and in behind-the-scenes confrontations over the formulation of Israeli policy on a variety of issues. In addition, Palti played a central role in managing Israel’s ties with friendly Sunni-Muslim countries: Jordan, Egypt, the United Arab Emirates and a few others that aren’t talked about publicly, even in the post-Abraham Accords era.

Palti, 58, a native of Ramat Hasharon, where he still lives, was drafted into the Intelligence Corps in November 1982, a few months after the start of the Lebanon War. In short order he found himself, as a noncom and afterward as a young officer, spending long periods in Lebanon, during which the Israel Defense Forces gradually pulled back to the Awali River line and afterward further south to the Litani River. 

“The [current] chief of staff, Aviv Kochavi, and I were in the same draft,” he relates. “Our generation in the army was shaped entirely in Lebanon. If I were to decide to undergo psychotherapy now, it would be in connection with that period. The sense of responsibility that was foisted on us as young officers was huge. That was the time, from the 1970s on, when terrorist squads infiltrated the Lebanon border and perpetrated attacks on Moshav Margaliot, Moshav Avivim and other places.

“There was this sign they put up in the operations room: ‘The mission – protecting the communities in the north.’ We took it with profound seriousness. If there were to be a penetration into a community in which children died, it would have been on our account. That’s a tremendous psychological burden for a guy of 21. I still get flashbacks from that period of patrol vehicles on the intrusion-tracking dirt road. You go to sleep wondering: Did I check that off the list or not?”

Another period immediately leaps to Palti’s mind. “Years later, in October 1992, a convoy of ours was hit by explosive devices near Kaukaba, in the eastern sector of the security zone. The soldiers traveled in armored Safari vehicles at the time, so Hezbollah hung fragmentation devices on electricity poles – which would enable them to strike high up, in the unarmored section of the truck. Five soldiers were killed. Kaplan [the nickname of Moshe Kaplinsky, later a major general and deputy chief of staff] was the commander of the territorial brigade, and the senior officer who arrived at the scene. He wanted to spare the young soldiers the grisly sight, so he moved the rest of the troops back and by himself evacuated the bodies of those who were killed in the truck.”

What Palti thinks today is that all that effort, with its multitudes of dead and wounded, was actually in vain – because the forces could have been moved south back across the border into Israel as early as the mid-1980s. “Four Mothers [a protest movement dedicated to bringing about an Israeli withdrawal from Lebanon] understood that before we did. Often there are civilian groups, whether left-wing or right-wing, that reveal other angles to you. You need to talk to them, listen to them, take into consideration that maybe they’re right. My decision to be part of the fortified wall that opposed an attack on nuclear sites in Iran in 2012 stems in part from the lessons I drew from the case of our remaining in Lebanon.”

This is the first time Palti has addressed publicly the dispute over whether to attack Iran. The usual focus is on the period of 2009-2011, when Benjamin Netanyahu as prime minister and Ehud Barak as defense minister pushed for an attack but encountered extensive opposition from top figures in the defense establishment. Palti was involved then, too, as head of the research division in the Mossad; in 2011, the agency’s new chief, Tamir Pardo, appointed him to the more senior position of head of the organization’s Intelligence Directorate, giving him a place near the head of the table. It was only in 2012, Palti says, that the internal debate in the Israeli security establishment reached a peak, “and someone will yet write books about those months.” 

“Ask those who were there,” he says. “I would start every intelligence survey presentation to the political decision makers by stating: The Iranians are not building a nuclear bomb. That drove Bibi and Ehud crazy. Why did I start by making that statement, which was based on solid information? Because if they’re not yet building a bomb, there is no urgency to attack.”

That assertion contradicted Barak’s theory of an Iranian “zone of immunity,” by which time the Iranians would have moved their means of production underground, thus safeguarding them against bombing and destruction. Barak believed it was urgent to attack before Iran reached that stage. Ten years later, he says, “one can judge who was right, Ehud or us. Cognitively, he’s the smartest person there is, which is exactly how he sees himself. To sit with him for hours is a real experience. He also has phenomenal rhetorical ability. On the other hand, I saw him and Netanyahu in 2012, and it was one of the most dangerous things that I’ve ever seen. We were in the room when it happened.”

Palti here confirms, in greater detail and with more color, accounts that have been mentioned since then by others who took part in the discussions, including Mossad heads Meir Dagan and Tamir Pardo, chiefs of staff Gabi Ashkenazi and Benny Gantz, and the Shin Bet security service director at the time, Yuval Diskin. This is testimony from within, from the inner sanctum, about one of the most dramatic chapters in Israel’s security history since the 1973 Yom Kippur War. Even though I have spoken about this with a number of those who were involved, and have read testimonies of others, I don’t think I have ever heard the matter voiced so bluntly.

“We would hold discussions in the ‘aquarium’ in the Prime Minister’s Office [the office set off by glass doors from the rest of the Prime Minister’s Office, in Jerusalem],” Palti relates, “and afterward we [security professionals] would go down to the parking area next to the bureau. Maj. Gen. Tal Russo, who was the head of the IDF’s Operations Directorate, would light a gas burner on the hood of his jeep and make black coffee. We stood there like that – Benny Gantz, Tamir, the IDF’s Planning Directorate head Amir Eshel, Military Intelligence head Kochavi and me – drinking coffee, looking at one another and asking: Hey, what just happened here? What do they want? Do they really intend to attack like that, out of the blue? 

“It’s 2012 that was the year of the major event, the real story,” he continues. “There’s no comparison between that and the first years of the dispute, with all their sound and fury. During the spring-summer of 2012, the operational preparations reached a peak. In my understanding, they [Netanyahu and Barak] were truly preparing to attack. I am not claiming to read the secrets of the heart. But when the IDF is instructed to deploy for ‘P+16’ [possible attack within 16 days], when air force aerial refueling planes are brought back from long-term servicing, it’s an emergency situation. The air force put all its planes into the air in order to prepare. Quite a few risks were taken in that period.”

Barak believed it was urgent to attack before Iran moved its nuclear production underground. Ten years later, Palti says, "one can judge who was right, Ehud or us … it was one of the most dangerous things that I’ve ever seen"

There are many people who are convinced to this day that they didn’t really intend to go ahead – that Netanyahu was incapable or ordering an attack and Barak didn’t want to, that it was all one big deception vis-à-vis the Americans and the world. 

“So they were just pulling the wool over the eyes of the chief of staff, the director of Military Intelligence, the commander of the air force? What did they gain from that? They carried out a deception operation at the expense of the chief of staff? I don’t buy that. To this day, there are arguments between the people involved about whether they really meant to [attack]. I’m certain that they did.”

Three people, according to Palti, lay themselves over the barbed-wire fence in order to express their objections to an attack: Pardo, Eshel and Palti himself. “Maybe I’m idealizing things a little in retrospect. But that was a red line for us. We all told them: If you issue an order, we will do everything to execute it.” But before that, they fought for their position. Apparently the strength of the opposition, almost wall to wall in the defense establishment hierarchy, was sufficient. Toward the fall of 2012, initial cracks appeared between Netanyahu and Barak. 

The defense minister gradually changed his mind, relying, among other sources, on a forecast by performance-analysis experts in the air force, who estimated that an Israeli attack on Iran would delay the latter’s nuclear project by a year and a half at most. Netanyahu effectively remained alone, in the face of American opposition and sweeping objections from the experts in Israel. The prime minister also did not have a majority who supported him in the “group of eight,” the select body of ministers that met frequently in order to deliberate the possibility of mounting an attack. 

In retrospect, some of Netanyahu’s supporters alleged that Meir Dagan, while in service, had deliberately betrayed secrets to the Americans in order to thwart the attack together with them.

“We did not go to tell the Americans. I think Dagan spoke to them with great openness, by virtue of his close relations with senior figures in the administration. But I don’t believe he gave them secret information that they didn’t know themselves,” notes Palti, who was very close both to Dagan and to his successor, Pardo. “What very much hastened the American progress toward an interim agreement with Iran, in 2013, and then the nuclear accord in 2015, was in part actually our behavior. We gave them a certain feeling of urgency [because of the threats of an attack], and that is what prompted the Obama administration to create a secret back channel of negotiations with Iran. A year later the interim agreement was signed, and the idea of an attack was dropped.”

Why, then, in contrast, had it been necessary to attack the reactor in Syria, in 2007?

“A significant difference exists between the threat that was posed by the Iranian nuclear project and the threat from the Syrian nuclear facility. In 2007, there was no real dilemma. From the moment the information arrived that the reactor in Syria would become hot [active and radioactive] within a few months, the [Ehud] Olmert government had no choice but to attack. That was also our recommendation – the IDF and the Mossad. And keep in mind the conditions under which we arrived at that decision. The IDF was still recovering from the 2006 war in Lebanon, Ehud Olmert was immersed in [criminal] investigations. And despite all that, we went for it despite the risk that a third Lebanon war would erupt. 

“There were no real questions, because it was obvious that it was essential. But what are you going to attack in Iran? An enrichment facility? I am in favor of attacking if there is an urgent need, if a sword is held to your throat, as Dagan put it. But that was not the situation in 2012 or in the preceding years.”

But what if you’re wrong and Netanyahu is right, and the right thing to do is to wage a fierce battle, including against the new nuclear accord that is now emerging?

“Similar arguments were made against me in 2012, too. I take into account that I might be wrong, and I ask myself that question almost every day. But let’s examine what happened between 2009 and 2012, the years in which the political decision makers wanted to attack the nuclear sites from the air. Did Iran acquire a bomb during those years? According to the foreign media, some of the delays in their program are related to things that Israel, and also other countries, apparently did in secret. Destructive malware, mysterious bombs, equipment breakdowns. There are things that can and should be done, other than an air attack.”

What will be the effect of the new agreement, which is apparently about to be signed in Vienna?

“From Israel’s point of view, both the previous agreement, and even more so the new one, are, in simple Hebrew, shitty. The new agreement doesn’t address all of our basic concerns, about the issues left unresolved [following the 2015 accord], the suspicions of previous Iranian violations, down to the nature of the supervision over the centrifuges. On the other hand, no one is actually asking us. In the eyes of the United States and the Europeans, what is important now is to stop the enrichment of uranium, before the Iranians move up from a 60-percent enrichment level to 90 percent. And they also want to remove the Iranian threat from the agenda and focus on what is urgent for them at the moment: China and Russia-Ukraine. 

“From the moment the agreement is signed, billions of dollars will flow into Iran. That money will not reach the civilian population, it will go first of all toward financing the terrorism of the Revolutionary Guards. The Americans need to understand that in the end they too will be dragged into having to contend with Iran again, whether because of Iranian terrorism or because clauses of the agreement will expire and policy will have to be formulated in the face of renewed progress in the nuclear project.”

‘I don’t want to see this’

Even though Palti is already talking as someone from whom a heavy weight of authority and responsibility has been removed, he does not use the opportunity of an interview to undertake a direct settling of accounts with the civilian policy makers. Apart from his description of the specific disagreement over the Iranian affair, he displays great esteem toward those in charge whom he worked with. And despite the differences of approach between right and left, he adds, one can discern without difficulty a national security conception that was accepted by all the prime ministers he worked under.

Our generation in the army was shaped entirely in Lebanon. If I were to decide to undergo psychotherapy now, it would be in that context. The sense of responsibility that was foisted on us as young officers was huge.Palti

“In 2002, I was head of the terrorism arena in Military Intelligence, at age 38. We brought the intelligence and the operational plan, which four years later would be translated into Operation Density in Lebanon, in which the IDF destroyed Hezbollah’s Fajr [long-range] missiles at the start of the war. I was sent to the prime minister, Ariel Sharon, to brief him in a meeting held in Ben-Gurion House [in Tel Aviv]. I presented everything to him in 45 minutes. He took everything down in a notebook, said to me, ‘Thank you very much, young colonel,’ turned to his military secretary, Kaplinsky, and said to him: I don’t want to see this in the near future. All the way back to the office in the Kirya [defense establishment headquarters in Tel Aviv], I thought: Why doesn’t he want to see it?

“Two years earlier, when Barak was still prime minister, Hezbollah abducted three soldiers on Har Dov [a mountain on the Israel-Lebanon border]. Barak had promised that if Hezbollah only tried to do something, the earth in Lebanon would tremble. But this time it was decided almost not to respond. Why, in both cases, did Barak and Sharon not take immediate action? My explanation is that they both thought and acted like military men who know how to manage risks. In the army there is a main effort and a secondary effort. Barak had just run into the intifada, Sharon was preparing for Operation Defensive Shield in the territories. They focused on what they saw as the main thing.

“Prime ministers in this country have to work vis-a-vis several clocks, which aren’t always synchronized. The entire Israeli public falls under their responsibility, but at the same time, they have to manage the country’s relations with the Americans, and also take world public opinion into consideration. Yes, including the Europeans – 30 percent of our trade takes place with Europe. There are leaderships in Germany, Britain, France that basically have our well-being good at heart. And there is also the question of the IDF’s preparedness: the condition of the inventory, the munitions, the level of training of the units. Those are not data that are available to the public, but prime ministers have to take them into account when making critical decisions. There are almost no political considerations in those decisions by prime ministers. 

“They must take into consideration urgent security problems. Nonetheless, I think a military background for a prime minister is absolutely not critical. Thinking like that is mistaken in a democracy. There is a strong professional system here that will readily support every leader, to help in making the best decisions possible. I was more favorably impressed by some of those with a civilian background than by the security people [who served as prime minister].”

In the IDF, and then in the Mossad, Palti worked alongside some of the most senior figures in the Israeli defense establishment of the past few decades. Among those who influenced him along the way, he mentions Maj. Gens. Kaplinsky and Matan Vilnai (Palti was the latter’s bureau chief as deputy chief of staff) and Mossad chiefs Dagan and Pardo. 

“Matan taught me how to plan on a large scale. From Kaplan I learned how to treat people under my command. With Dagan I had a deep, inexplicable connection. He recruited me to the Mossad with one phone call, at 11:30 at night. It was impossible to say no to him. The state owes to him and to Tamir the success of the operation to destroy the Syrian reactor. I remember a discussion in which Pardo raised an objection to a suggestion that came up, and people didn’t take in what he wanted. Ehud Barak stopped the discussion and said: ‘I want you to listen to Tamir. Having commanded certain operational units in the Mossad, he has experience that none of the rest of us have.’ In general, I think that pluralism is a very strong characteristic of the Israeli system. That was so with Dagan and Pardo, also with Barak and Netanyahu, and also with the defense ministers I worked with: Lieberman, Bennett and Gantz. 

“The pluralism of opinions in the room is one of the system’s great assets. In the end, in major issues, state considerations are decisive. In the dispute over Iran in 2012, too, it’s not that we won and they [Netanyahu and Barak] lost. I am pleased that they did not make the decision to attack, but if they had done so, we would have executed it.”

‘Amazing achievement’

Palti, who for years played a central role in Israel’s secret ties with the Gulf states, sees the Abraham Accords, which Netanyahu signed in the fall of 2020, establishing diplomatic relations with both the United Arab Emirates and Bahrain, as an “amazing achievement. People in Israel don’t understand it sufficiently. I am still apprehensive that we will screw it up. A national project coordinator needs to be assigned to [cultivate] those relations, one who will look at the whole picture and synchronize the activity of government ministries. There are allegations that the Defense Ministry is supposedly dragging its feet in authorizing security transactions with the UAE and Bahrain. I think it’s better that way. I didn’t think it was necessary to rush to be first in the security realm. The other side didn’t want that, either. They wanted to establish civilian contracts in the economic, high-tech, desalination realms. That’s what rivets them. 

“We are building agreements here for years, and peace is established through relations between people. That is the lesson of the peace agreements with Egypt and Jordan, which were not translated into ties between the peoples. The masses of Israeli tourists in Dubai – that’s what’s important in my eyes. The security industries in Israel are complaining that we lost five months [after the agreements were signed], but in my opinion it was necessary so that there will be order in the system. Now things have started to move. The government bears tremendous responsibility, to move ahead and develop the relations with them and to preserve the Abraham Accords. That is the future. I hope we won’t wake up in another few years and say that we blew those relations. 

“Our interlocutors in the Gulf measure us by several criteria. They admire Israeli technology and the achievements of the security industries. The Arrow missiles, Iron Dome, satellites – those are the brands everyone wants to have. The images of civilians in restaurants with missiles being fired around them, the photograph that looks like ‘Star Wars’ – when Iron Dome intercepts volleys above Ashkelon – impressed them very much. But contrary to the reports, we have not sold Iron Dome batteries to any country. We gave one battery to the Americans, but they didn’t transfer it to other countries.”

He notes that “our closeness to the United States is a huge lever of influence, a tremendous asset in the face of the Arab states. Many times I sat in closed rooms [with Arab officials], and messages to the Americans were transferred through me. America is the most significant foundation in our national security.”

Palti’s esteem for the Americans is evident throughout my conversations with him. He has professional ties of many years with senior figures in successive administrations, from the Defense Department and the State Department to the various intelligence agencies. Last week, during a farewell visit to the Pentagon, he was awarded the Department of Defense Medal for Distinguished Public Service, one of the most prestigious honors the administration can award to foreign nationals. The medal, said Under Secretary of Defense Colin Kahl, was given to Palti for “his significant contribution to strengthening the strategic security ties between the two countries.”

“There is no substitute for the United States,” Palti says. “Anyone in Israel who is toying with the idea of moving away from American influence is making a fundamental mistake. In my last job, I worked with two administrations, Republican and Democrat, and both saw equally to ensuring Israel’s qualitative edge. We have no friend like them in the world. From the F-35 to the M-16, almost all the weapons we acquire are manufactured by them and purchased with American security aid funds. The White House and the majority of Congress are on our side. As is public opinion. That is based on values the two countries share: democracy, human dignity, freedom of expression. We must preserve that for all we’re worth. I am also hearing other voices, in the progressive camp, and I find that disturbing. We are blessed to have them on our side, and we must not take it for granted. We should always be on the side of the Americans. They are a nation that develops vaccines, is looking for medications for cancer, sends astronauts into space – overall, they are looking out for the good of humanity.”

That identification need not be translated into a strategic defense pact with the United States, in his view, along the lines that Netanyahu considered – whether as a gimmick or with serious intent – amid the recent spate of elections. “The possibility of our relying on ourselves is an acute security need. It’s not by chance that we refrained from [entering into] a defense pact all these years. You don’t want someone telling you: Don’t attack in Syria now, or for Congress to prevent you from attacking because you are liable to endanger the lives of American soldiers in the future.”

Palti demurs, cautiously, from comments voiced in Netanyahu’s circle in his last years in office to the effect that Israel can rely only on the support of Orthodox Jews and the Christian evangelicals in the United States, and that it is impossible to count on the support of Reform Jewry. “We cannot forgo any Jew anywhere. I don’t accept the claim that we lost them long ago. We have to fight for the bond with each of them. The crisis with American Jewry over the Western Wall arrangements is in my view a strategic issue for Israel’s national security. It has to be resolved.”

Another source of strength for Israel, Palti says, stems from the fact that the neighboring countries see Israelis as people who dare, who don’t think twice when an offensive action is necessary. “From their point of view, there is a certain aura about us, which emanates from the destruction of the Iraqi reactor in 1981 and the Syrian reactor in 2007. They also took the same attitude toward the preparations for attacking Iran and for the attacks in the ‘campaign between the wars,’ which is now being waged in Syria. They appreciate our ability to quarrel with the U.S. administration when necessary. Netanyahu made a mistake, in my view, when he addressed Congress against the nuclear agreement in 2015, but that move won him a lot of points in the Arab states.”

Where we were wrong

The solution will be confined to Judea and Samaria. With Hamas there’s nothing to discuss. They don’t recognize us and can’t be part of the solution if they don’t revise that stance.Palti

Anyone who seeks to locate Palti on the left side of the political map, given his opposition to attacking Iran and his view of the essential character of relations with the Sunni Arab countries, may be surprised to hear his views on the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. “The Oslo process died long ago,” he says. “That is not a political position, it’s my professional opinion. There were two main reasons for that. The first is the loss of mutual trust caused by the second intifada, with the suicide attacks – damage that will be very difficult to repair. The second is Hamas’ takeover of the Gaza Strip in 2007, two years after the ‘disengagement.’ 

“It’s impossible now to talk about the realization of the two-state vision,” he continues, “because there are actually three entities here, and that’s how things are unfolding. The Gaza Strip is a completely separate entity, and the main thing that’s preventing union between the West Bank and Gaza is the harsh internal rivalry between the Palestinian Authority and Hamas.

“Does that mean that the two-state solution should not remain part of the public debate? No. But in any event, the solution will be confined to Judea and Samaria. With Hamas there is nothing to talk about regarding a true political process. That organization does not recognize us and can’t be part of the solution as long as it is does not revise its basic stance. Our strategy now rests largely on the understanding that at this stage the situation is not ripe for a settlement that will include concrete concessions, not from the Palestinian point of view and not for most citizens of Israel. There is no possibility at the moment of furthering the two-state solution. 

“Much has changed on the ground: the roads, the demography. It’s impossible to cling to the past. We will need to adjust the discussion to the reality that’s going to exist on the ground for another few years. We are in a status quo [situation] until the Israeli public will decide otherwise. It’s clear to me that a single state will be a disaster. The Palestinians are talking about that a lot lately, and that talk worries me.”

Since the Israeli pullout from Gaza [in 2005], Israel has launched four military operations in the Strip and almost nothing has changed. Where did we go wrong?

“Our dilemma in Gaza is [deciding] between continuation of the existing policy and a broader opening of the Strip – perhaps not as far as Shimon Peres’ Singapore vision. Billions of dollars from the international community have flowed into Gaza. At the moment, Hamas is not sufficiently looking after the residents there. More money is being invested in building up its military force than in water, sewerage and electricity infrastructure. It’s not Israel that delayed the establishment of a desalination plant in the Gaza Strip; Hamas is not ensuring that the children there have normal water to drink.”

Our conflict with Gaza won’t be over soon, Palti says, citing his own family’s story as proof. “In the ‘50s, my father, Uri, was a member of the Nahal group that founded Kibbutz Nahal Oz, on the Gaza border. He had a friend, Roi Rotberg, who was the security coordinator of the kibbutz. One morning, Rotberg went out on patrol along the furrow that marked the border. Palestinians kidnapped him and killed him. His horse came back to the kibbutz on her own, and Roi’s body was returned only the next day, via UN personnel, after being subjected to serious abuse. The chief of staff, Moshe Dayan, made his famous eulogy over Roi’s grave. A decade later, my father took part in the conquest of Gaza during the Six-Day War. Two decades later, I served as an intelligence officer in Golani during the First Intifada. And now, more than 30 years later, my son is an intelligence officer in the Gaza division.” 

Palti sounds far more optimistic when he talks about the ties with Jordan and Egypt, two countries he has visited dozens of times over the years, albeit far from the media’s eye. “Relations with both are among the greatest strategic assets we possess,” he says. “We have survived so many bumps in the road with them. There is hardly any sort of crisis we haven’t gone through with them. The rise to power of President Abdel-Fatah al-Sissi upgraded all the relations with Egypt, after difficult years.”

In 2012, in the midst of Operation Pillar of Defense, in Gaza, Netanyahu sent Palti and Mossad head Pardo to Cairo, to wrap up a cease-fire agreement with Hamas with the help of Egypt. The Egyptian government was then being led, for a short time, by the Muslim Brotherhood, following the toppling of the regime of Hosni Mubarak. 

Palti: “Listen, to drive through the streets of Cairo in an unarmored Peugeot 406 under the rule of the Muslim Brotherhood is a formative experience. You’re used to visiting there with a secure convoy, but at that time they were afraid to show that they were in contact with us. Things improved immediately when Sissi came to power. 

“The Americans made a lot of mistakes regarding Egypt, but in the end the triangular relationship between them and us and Egypt is critical. It must be preserved no matter what. Regarding Jordan, I am familiar with all the complaints against them, but what they give us is of tremendous value. In the end, that is our longest border, and the IDF barely even needs to invest in guarding it. The Jordanians see to that and provide us with strategic depth.”

In the past month, Haaretz quoted senior sources in the defense establishment who urged reconsidering the possibility of an offensive campaign against Hezbollah, given the apprehension that the Shi’ite organization has vastly improved the accuracy of its arsenal of rockets in Lebanon. Palti read those remarks and is against the idea. “I suggest to the whole generation who knew not Lebanon to take a deep breath and show restraint. You don’t mess with Lebanon if there’s no need. Only if there is necessity and a threat will we need to act,” he says.

“In Lebanon, the situation is one of ‘zero or one hundred.’ If you get into a war there, you will get to the point where you are destroying cities. Therefore, we suggest to the government of Lebanon to prevent that process, not to get to that point. But when there are recommendations from the IDF to attack in Lebanon like in Syria, in a campaign between the wars, I find that to be a bad strategic idea. We have no real border dispute with Lebanon, a compromise can be found for the gas reserves in the Mediterranean, and the dispute over the exact place where the land border will run is resolvable. On the other hand, the potential threat from there is immense. As long as they don’t strike at our civilians, there are no grounds for a war against them. That is something we learned over the years: You go to war only when there is no choice. That is a central element of our security conception.”

Between the two conversations I held with Palti, Russia invaded Ukraine and Europe found itself in the most serious security crisis since the collapse of the Soviet bloc at the beginning of the 1990s. Palti urges Israeli caution on this issue and supports the line taken by Prime Minister Bennett. “We are doing a great deal to assist in the humanitarian sphere. Some are trying to drag us into direct involvement in the confrontation itself, but Israel doesn’t have to occupy a place at the center of the stage in every conflict. Forces far larger than us are involved here: Russia, the United States, the countries of Europe, NATO. I am appalled when I see the heartbreaking sights from the war, but I don’t think that our taking a side will turn things around. Everything that is happening there will have immediate implications for the situation here, and it is impossible to ignore the fact that lately we have a big neighbor [Russia] on our northern border.”

One lesson for Israel, he avers, will be to preserve its technological military advantage. “We see now what is happening in Ukraine, when they are compelled to mobilize weapons systems from here and from there, and learn how to use them. An army needs years to absorb advanced weapons systems. In light of the threats, Israel must constantly be on the cutting edge of technology, both in the development of missile-intercept capabilities, including lasers, and also with offensive ability.”

This will obligate an increase in the Israeli defense budget, he says, as will also be the case in many European countries, which can be expected to make large-scale purchases from the Israeli military industries. “The economy here is quite thriving because the security situation is relatively good,” he observes. “Routine security activity and the larger operations are very costly, but in the end they also boost the economy. In this environment, the security activity ensures you a level of quiet and stability. People have short memories. They tend to forget what happened here during the second intifada and how that affected the economy.
